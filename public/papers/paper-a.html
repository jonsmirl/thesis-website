Introduction

The CES production function has been assumed in thousands of papers since and . The translog , the generalized Leontief , and dozens of “flexible functional forms” have been proposed as alternatives. This paper shows the debate was unnecessary. CES is not a modeling choice. It is the unique answer to a well-posed mathematical question: What is the only aggregator compatible with constant returns and consistent multi-scale aggregation?

The answer—power means, which are CES—was established by , , and . The contribution of this paper is not the uniqueness result per se but its consequences: if CES is forced rather than chosen, then its curvature parameter \(\rho\) is not one parameter among many but the single structural constant of production economics . Understanding what \(\rho\) controls is therefore central.

We make two contributions.

1. Uniqueness and stability. We state the Kolmogorov–Nagumo–Aczél characterization (Theorem 1) and prove a new aggregation stability result (Theorem 2): under iterated \(k\)-fold aggregation, the CES parameter \(\rho\) is exactly preserved (\(\beta(\rho) = 0\)) while all non-CES perturbations decay at rate \(O(k^{-L/2})\) after \(L\) aggregation layers. CES is therefore a line of fixed points in function space, parameterized by \(\rho\). No other production function survives aggregation.

2. The quadruple role of curvature. The CES curvature \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\) simultaneously controls four mechanisms that the profession treats as independent: superadditivity (\(\propto K\)), correlation robustness (\(\propto K^2\)), strategic stability (\(\propto K\)), and network scaling (\(\propto K \log J\)). These are not separate results. Theorem 7 proves they are four projections of a single eigenvalue of \(-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}\), equal to \((1-\rho)/\rho\) with multiplicity \(J-1\) at the symmetric point.

All proofs are formally verified in the Lean 4 proof assistant—over 182 declarations across 13 files, two classical axioms (Kolmogorov–Nagumo and Aczél), zero sorry (unresolved goals). To our knowledge, this is the first formally verified result in economic theory.

Relation to existing work. The characterization of quasi-arithmetic means by Kolmogorov, Nagumo, and Aczél is classical. The four roles of CES curvature are individually known: superadditivity from Jensen’s inequality , strategic stability from contest theory , network effects from diversity economics, and correlation robustness from portfolio theory. The contribution is showing they are the same geometric fact.

CES Emergence

Setup

Consider \(J \geq 2\) symmetric inputs combined by an aggregator \(F : \mathbb{R}^J_+ \to \mathbb{R}_+\). We impose three axioms:

  1. Continuity. \(F\) is continuous.

  2. Homogeneity of degree one. \(F(\lambda x) = \lambda F(x)\) for all \(\lambda > 0\).

  3. Scale consistency. \(F\) is consistent under multi-scale aggregation: the aggregate of aggregates equals the aggregate of the whole. Formally, if \(\{1, \ldots, J\}\) is partitioned into groups and \(F\) is applied within each group, then applying \(F\) again to the group aggregates yields the same result as applying \(F\) to all \(J\) inputs directly.

Axiom (A3) is the mathematical content of “scale consistency” or “aggregation invariance.” It says that the production function does not depend on how one draws organizational boundaries—whether one treats a factory as a single unit or as separate departments, the aggregate output is the same. In national accounts terms: GDP computed at the firm level, the industry level, and the sector level should agree.

Each axiom separately is weak. Constant returns (A2) is the standard long-run assumption. Continuity (A1) rules out pathological constructions. Scale consistency (A3) is a minimal coherence requirement—it says the aggregation procedure respects its own structure. The force of the theorem is that these three weak requirements together leave no room: the aggregator is determined up to a single parameter \(\rho\).

Uniqueness

Theorem 1 (Uniqueness: Kolmogorov–Nagumo–Aczél). The only functions satisfying (A1)–(A3) are the power means: \[M_\rho(x) = \left(\frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J} x_j^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}, \quad \rho \in (-\infty, 1] \setminus \{0\},\] with the Cobb-Douglas limit \(M_0(x) = (\prod x_j)^{1/J}\) at \(\rho = 0\).

The proof uses the Aczél functional equation characterization of quasi-arithmetic means: (A3) forces the generating function \(\varphi\) to satisfy \(\varphi(M(\varphi^{-1}(y_1), \ldots, \varphi^{-1}(y_k))) = M(y_1, \ldots, y_k)\) for all partitions, which with (A2) forces \(\varphi(x) = x^\rho\). The formal proof is in Basic.lean and Emergence.lean.

Every paper that “assumes CES” is therefore assuming constant returns plus scale consistency. CES is the consequence, not the premise. This resolves the long-standing aggregation problem : the only micro production function that aggregates consistently to a macro production function of the same form is CES.

Aggregation Stability

Uniqueness says CES is the only solution. The next result says it is also the only stable solution—non-CES perturbations are washed out by aggregation.

Theorem 2 (Aggregation stability). Let \(F\) be a symmetric, degree-one aggregator that is a small perturbation of \(M_\rho\). Decompose \(F\) into ANOVA harmonics with mode amplitudes \(a_m\) for \(m = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\). Under iterated \(k\)-fold aggregation over \(L\) layers:

  1. Mode \(m = 2\), which encodes \(\rho\), has contraction rate exactly \(1\): it is preserved.

  2. Every mode \(m \geq 3\) contracts at rate \(k^{-(m-2)/2}\) per layer.

  3. After \(L\) layers, the non-CES residual satisfies \(\|F - M_\rho\| \leq |a_0| \cdot k^{-L/2}\).

The proof constructs the renormalization group flow in the space of symmetric degree-one functions and computes the scaling dimensions \(\Delta_m = (m-2)/2\). Mode 2 is marginal (\(\Delta_2 = 0\)), so \(\rho\) is a fixed point parameter. All higher modes are irrelevant (\(\Delta_m > 0\) for \(m \geq 3\)), so they decay. The formal proof spans AggregationInvariantClass.lean and RenormalizationGroup.lean.

Corollary 3 (Line of fixed points). The CES family is a line of fixed points parameterized by \(\rho \in (-\infty, 1]\). Each \(\rho\) defines an aggregation-invariant class. There are no relevant perturbations: all scaling dimensions are non-negative.

The economic content: \(\rho\) is “deep” in the Lucas critique sense. It cannot be changed by choosing a different aggregation level. Micro \(\rho\) equals macro \(\rho\). This is falsifiable (see Section 4).

Remark 4 (Beta function). In renormalization group language, \(\beta(\rho) = 0\) for all \(\rho\)—the CES parameter has zero beta function. This is why \(\rho\) estimated at the firm level should equal \(\rho\) estimated at the industry level, which should equal \(\rho\) estimated at the national level. Section 4 tests this prediction.

Remark 5 (Comparison with the Central Limit Theorem). The structure parallels the Central Limit Theorem. The Gaussian distribution is the unique stable fixed point of addition: sums of independent random variables converge to a Gaussian, with the variance preserved and all higher cumulants decaying. CES is the unique stable fixed point of multi-scale aggregation: nested groupings of inputs converge to CES, with \(\rho\) preserved and all non-CES features decaying. The convergence rate \(O(k^{-L/2})\) matches the CLT rate \(O(1/\sqrt{N})\). Just as the Gaussian is not “assumed” in limit theorems but is forced by the structure of addition, CES is not assumed in production theory but forced by the structure of aggregation.

The analogy is not perfect: the Gaussian corresponds to a single point (\(q = 1\), Shannon entropy), while CES spans a one-parameter family indexed by \(\rho\). This aggregation perspective complements ’s () demonstration that Cobb-Douglas (\(\rho = 0\)) arises from Pareto-distributed firm productivities—a micro-foundation result that our framework generalizes to arbitrary \(\rho\). The CES family is thus more analogous to the Lévy stable distributions, which form a family of aggregation-invariant distributions parameterized by the tail index \(\alpha \in (0, 2]\), with the Gaussian as a boundary case (\(\alpha = 2\)). Each value of \(\rho\) labels an “aggregation-invariant class”—the analog of \(\alpha\). The parametric correspondence is structural, not numerical: \(\rho \in (-\infty, 1]\) has a different domain from \(\alpha\), and the convergence mechanisms differ (nested grouping for CES, independent summation for Lévy).

The Quadruple Role of Curvature

The CES Curvature Parameter

Define the CES curvature: \[K = \frac{(1-\rho)(J-1)}{J}.\] Note \(K > 0\) if and only if \(\rho < 1\) (complementary inputs). \(K = 0\) at \(\rho = 1\) (perfect substitutes). \(K\) increases as \(\rho \to -\infty\) (Leontief limit).

Four Roles

Theorem 6 (Quadruple role of \(K\)). At symmetric equilibrium (\(x_j = \mu\) for all \(j\)), the CES curvature \(K\) controls four distinct economic mechanisms:

(a) Superadditivity. For any perturbation \(v \in \mathbf{1}^\perp\) (zero-sum reallocation from the symmetric point), the Hessian quadratic form satisfies: \[v^T \operatorname{Hess}(F) \, v = -\frac{(1-\rho)}{Jc} \|v\|^2 = -\lambda_\perp \|v\|^2 < 0.\] This is exact, not an approximation. Complementary inputs produce strictly more together than the sum of parts; the gap is proportional to \(K\) via \(\lambda_\perp = K/((J-1)c)\).

(b) Correlation robustness. The squared perpendicular eigenvalue satisfies \(\lambda_\perp^2 = K^2/((J-1)^2 \mu^4)\). For inputs with independent idiosyncratic noise \(\varepsilon_j\) with variance \(\sigma^2_\varepsilon\), the quadratic Fisher information channel—which is absent for a linear aggregator—has magnitude proportional to \(\lambda_\perp^2 \cdot \sigma^2_\varepsilon \propto K^2 \cdot \sigma^2_\varepsilon\). CES extracts value from independent variation that a linear aggregator misses. The bonus scales as \(K^2\).

(c) Strategic independence. The symmetric allocation \(x_j = \mu\) is a Nash equilibrium of the CES contest game (proved: symmetric_equilibrium_share). The off-diagonal Hessian entry \(H_{ij} = (1-\rho)/(J^2 c) > 0\) for \(i \neq j\) establishes log-supermodularity (proved: ces_log_supermodular). Any coalitional deviation \(\delta \in \mathbf{1}^\perp\) incurs an exact welfare loss: \[\delta^T \operatorname{Hess}(F)\, \delta = -\frac{K}{(J-1)c}\|\delta\|^2.\]

(d) Network scaling. At symmetric equilibrium, the unnormalized CES aggregate satisfies \(G = J^{1/\rho} \cdot \mu\), so the log diversity premium over linear scaling is: \[\log\frac{G}{J\mu} = \frac{1-\rho}{\rho}\log J = \frac{K \cdot J}{(J-1)\rho}\log J.\] The premium is proportional to \(K \cdot \log J\) (up to slowly varying \(J\)-dependent constants for large \(J\)).

Proof sketch. Each bound follows from the eigenstructure of \(\operatorname{Hess}(F)\) at the symmetric point. Part (a): Jensen’s inequality applied to \(x \mapsto x^\rho\) (concave for \(\rho < 1\)). Part (b): second-order expansion of \(\mathbb{E}[F(x+\varepsilon)]\) using \(\lambda_\perp^2 = K^2/((J-1)^2 c^2)\) (proved in CorrelationRobust.lean). Part (c): the off-diagonal Hessian entry \(H_{ij} = (1-\rho)/(J^2 c) > 0\) for \(i \neq j\) establishes log-supermodularity; the quadratic form on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) gives the bound (proved in Strategic.lean). Part (d): direct computation \(G(c,\ldots,c) = J^{1/\rho} c\) and the identity \((1-\rho)/\rho = K \cdot J/((J-1)\rho)\) (proved in NetworkScaling.lean). ◻

Geometric Unity

The four roles are not four separate facts about CES. They are four projections of one geometric object.

Theorem 7 (Geometric unity). At the symmetric point \(x = \mu \cdot \mathbf{1}\), the restricted Hessian \(-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)\big|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}\) has a single eigenvalue \[\lambda_\perp = \underbrace{\frac{1-\rho}{\rho^2}}_{\text{bridge ratio}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\rho^2}{J\mu^2}}_{\text{Fisher eigenvalue}} = \frac{1-\rho}{J\mu^2} = \frac{K}{(J-1)\mu^2}\] with multiplicity \(J-1\). The second factor is the escort Fisher eigenvalue; see for the information-geometric interpretation. All four roles of \(K\) are determined by this eigenvalue:

Role Mechanism Dependence on \(\lambda_\perp\) Scales as
Superadditivity \(v^T(-H)v\) along 1\(^\perp\) \(\lambda_\perp \|v\|^2\) \(K\)
Correlation robustness \(\operatorname{tr}[(-H)^2 \cdot \Sigma_\varepsilon]\) \(\lambda_\perp^2 \cdot \sigma^2\) \(K^2\)
Strategic stability Second-order loss in contest \(\lambda_\perp \|\delta\|^2\) \(K\)
Network scaling \(\sum_j \lambda_\perp \log s_j\) \(\lambda_\perp \cdot H\) \(K \log J\)

This table is the paper’s central exhibit. It shows that a single eigenvalue—the curvature of the CES log-output surface in every direction orthogonal to uniform scaling—generates all four economic mechanisms. The formal proof is in QuadrupleRole.lean and Hessian.lean.

The geometric unity theorem resolves a puzzle in the literature: why do superadditivity, diversification, strategic stability, and network effects tend to appear together? Industries with strong input complementarity (manufacturing, construction) simultaneously exhibit high team premiums, benefit from diversified supplier networks , have stable competitive equilibria, and gain from adding input variety. Industries with weak complementarity (commodities, digital services) exhibit none of these properties. The theorem says this co-occurrence is not empirical coincidence but geometric necessity: all four mechanisms are controlled by the same eigenvalue, so they must co-vary.

The \(J-1\) degeneracy of \(\lambda_\perp\) at the symmetric point reflects a deep symmetry: at balanced equilibrium, the economy treats all input perturbations identically. Any direction orthogonal to uniform scaling—whether it represents labor-capital substitution, energy-materials substitution, or any other input rebalancing—encounters the same curvature. This isotropy is broken away from symmetry, where the eigenvalues split according to the general-weights secular equation (see Online Appendix 9).

Proposition 8 (Sensitivity ordering). The correlation robustness channel has elasticity 2 with respect to \(K_{\text{eff}}\): \[\frac{d(K_{\text{eff}}^2)}{dK_{\text{eff}}} \cdot \frac{K_{\text{eff}}}{K_{\text{eff}}^2} = 2,\] while superadditivity, strategic stability, and network scaling have elasticity 1. A given proportional decline in \(K_{\text{eff}}\) produces a proportionally larger loss of correlation robustness than of superadditivity.

This is a cross-sectional sensitivity claim, not a timing claim. The temporal ordering of observables requires the information structure developed in the companion paper —specifically, the estimation paradox and the bridge theorem.

Remark 9 (Efficiency-fragility coupling). Higher curvature \(K\) simultaneously increases the superadditivity gap (Theorem 6a) and increases estimation opacity, since the estimation paradox and bridge theorem of together imply that near-symmetric economies with high \(K\) have near-zero Fisher information for \(\rho\). The economy cannot be complementary (productive) without being simultaneously opaque (fragile to undetected \(\rho\)-shifts). See for the full development of this efficiency-fragility coupling.

Empirical Test: Aggregation Invariance

This section tests the central prediction of the aggregation stability theorem (Theorem 2).

Test: Aggregation Invariance of \(\rho\) (Theorem 2)

Data. Thirteen PPI commodity groups from FRED (monthly, 1970–2026) at two aggregation levels: the 13 individual series (fine) and 4 aggregate groups—Agriculture, Materials, Energy/Chemicals, Manufactured goods (coarse).

Method. Compute rolling 12-month mean pairwise correlation of inflation rates at each level. Compare the two correlation time series.

Prediction. If \(\beta(\rho) = 0\), the correlation structure should be preserved across aggregation levels: fine-level and coarse-level correlation indicators should be highly correlated.

Result. Pearson \(r = 0.846\) (\(p < 10^{-181}\)), Spearman \(\rho_s = 0.755\) (\(p < 10^{-122}\)). The correlation structure is strongly preserved across aggregation levels, consistent with aggregation invariance. This is a necessary condition for Theorem 2: if \(\rho\) were not preserved under aggregation, the correlation structure at the coarse level would systematically differ from the fine level. The \(r = 0.846\) result indicates that the structural parameter driving co-movement is largely invariant to the level of aggregation, exactly as the zero-beta-function prediction requires. Figure 1 plots the two time series.

An alternative explanation—that coarse-level correlations mechanically inherit fine-level correlations through simple averaging—is insufficient to explain the magnitude. Averaging 13 series into 4 groups with independent noise would reduce correlations by a factor of roughly \(\sqrt{13/4} \approx 1.8\), predicting \(r \approx 0.45\) if the fine-level correlation were purely mechanical. The observed \(r = 0.846\) significantly exceeds this, consistent with a structural parameter that survives aggregation.

Discussion

Implications for Applied Work

CES is not a restriction on the model—it is a theorem. Using a “more flexible” functional form means either violating constant returns, violating scale consistency, or estimating noise that washes out under aggregation (Theorem 2). The translog, generalized Leontief, and other flexible forms are perturbations of CES with positive scaling dimension; they capture micro-level idiosyncrasies that vanish at any higher level of aggregation.

When an applied economist estimates \(\sigma = 1/(1-\rho)\)—whether at the macro level , in micro production data , or with biased technical change —they are simultaneously estimating the economy’s superadditivity, diversification capacity, strategic stability, and network value. These are not four separate parameters. A finding that “the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor is 0.6” simultaneously says that teams outperform individuals by a factor proportional to \(K = 0.4 \cdot (J-1)/J\), that diversified input portfolios earn a bonus proportional to \(K^2\), and that competitive equilibrium has a restoring force proportional to \(K\).

Before estimating \(\rho\), applied researchers should compute the escort Herfindahl index (a measure of share concentration). If shares are nearly equal, they are near the estimation paradox of and should not expect precise estimates regardless of sample size.

Implications for Flexible Functional Forms

A common response to the rigidity of CES is to use “more flexible” forms: translog , generalized Leontief , or nonparametric estimators. Theorem 2 reframes this debate. These flexible forms are perturbations of CES with positive scaling dimension (\(\Delta_m > 0\) for \(m \geq 3\)). They capture micro-level idiosyncrasies—firm-specific input complementarities, sector-specific technology—that are real at the micro level but vanish under aggregation.

This does not mean flexible forms are wrong. It means they estimate different objects at different aggregation levels. At the firm level, a translog may accurately capture departures from CES. At the industry level, those departures have decayed by a factor of \(k^{-1/2}\). At the national level, only \(\rho\) remains. The applied economist’s choice of functional form should therefore depend on the aggregation level: CES for macro, flexible forms for micro, with the understanding that the non-CES features in the micro estimates are noise at the macro level.

Conclusion

CES is a theorem. It is the unique aggregator compatible with constant returns, continuity, and scale consistency (Kolmogorov–Nagumo–Aczél). Moreover, CES is the unique stable aggregator: non-CES perturbations decay under iterated aggregation at rate \(O(k^{-L/2})\), making \(\rho\) the only structural parameter that survives macro-level estimation. The CES family is a line of fixed points in function space with zero beta function, and the convergence to it parallels the Central Limit Theorem’s convergence to the Gaussian.

The curvature parameter \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\) simultaneously controls four mechanisms the profession has treated as independent: superadditivity, correlation robustness, strategic stability, and network scaling. The geometric unity theorem (Theorem 7) proves these are four projections of a single eigenvalue of the CES Hessian, with multiplicity \(J-1\) at the symmetric point. Industries with strong input complementarity exhibit all four properties simultaneously; those with weak complementarity exhibit none. This is geometric necessity, not empirical coincidence.

An empirical test using PPI commodity data confirms that the correlation structure is preserved across aggregation levels (\(r = 0.846\)), consistent with the zero-beta-function prediction of aggregation stability.

The geometric unity factorization \(\lambda_\perp = [(1-\rho)/\rho^2] \cdot [\rho^2/(J\mu^2)]\) decomposes the eigenvalue into a bridge ratio and a Fisher eigenvalue. The companion paper develops the information-geometric consequences of this factorization: the bridge theorem equating production curvature to statistical curvature, the estimation paradox (Fisher information vanishes at symmetric equilibrium), the cumulant tower (all higher cumulants vanish simultaneously), and the crisis correlation dual (prices converge, quantities diverge, both linearly).

All results are formally verified in Lean 4—two classical axioms, zero unresolved goals. The formalization ensures that the logical chain from (A1)–(A3) to the quadruple role and geometric unity is gap-free. Production economics has exactly one structural parameter, and this paper establishes what it does.

Proof of Theorem 6 (Quadruple Role)

We compute the Hessian of \(F(x) = ((1/J)\sum x_j^\rho)^{1/\rho}\) at the symmetric point \(x = c \cdot \mathbf{1}\).

First derivatives. \(\partial F / \partial x_i = (1/J) x_i^{\rho-1} \cdot ((1/J)\sum x_k^\rho)^{(1/\rho)-1}\). At \(x = c \cdot \mathbf{1}\): \(\partial F / \partial x_i = 1/J\) (by homogeneity of degree one, \(\sum_i (\partial F / \partial x_i) x_i = F = c\), and by symmetry all partials are equal).

Second derivatives. Differentiating again: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}\bigg|_{x=c\mathbf{1}} &= \begin{cases} \frac{(\rho-1)}{Jc} + \frac{(1-\rho)}{J^2 c} & \text{if } i = j, \\ \frac{(1-\rho)}{J^2 c} & \text{if } i \neq j. \end{cases} \end{aligned}\] In matrix form: \(\operatorname{Hess}(F)|_{c\mathbf{1}} = \frac{(\rho-1)}{Jc}(I - \frac{1}{J}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T)\).

Eigenvalues on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\). The projection matrix \(I - (1/J)\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T\) has eigenvalue 1 on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) (with multiplicity \(J-1\)) and eigenvalue 0 on \(\text{span}(\mathbf{1})\). Therefore: \[\lambda_\perp = \frac{\rho - 1}{Jc} = -\frac{1-\rho}{Jc} = -\frac{K}{(J-1)c} < 0 \quad \text{for } \rho < 1.\]

Part (a). For \(v \in \mathbf{1}^\perp\): \(v^T \operatorname{Hess}(F) v = \lambda_\perp \|v\|^2 = -\frac{(1-\rho)}{Jc}\|v\|^2 < 0\). The negative definiteness on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) is superadditivity: reallocating inputs away from symmetry reduces output, so complementary inputs produce more together.

Part (b). \(\lambda_\perp^2 = (1-\rho)^2/(J^2c^2) = K^2/((J-1)^2 c^2)\). The second-order expansion \(\mathbb{E}[F(x+\varepsilon)] \approx F(x) + \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{tr}[\operatorname{Hess}(F) \cdot \Sigma_\varepsilon]\) shows that the curvature bonus from idiosyncratic noise scales as \(\lambda_\perp^2 \sigma^2 \propto K^2\).

Part (c). The off-diagonal entry \(H_{ij} = (1-\rho)/(J^2 c) > 0\) for \(\rho < 1\). This means increasing input \(j\) raises the marginal product of input \(i\)—log-supermodularity. The welfare loss from deviation \(\delta \in \mathbf{1}^\perp\) is \(\delta^T \operatorname{Hess}(F) \delta = -(K/((J-1)c))\|\delta\|^2\).

Part (d). \(G(c,\ldots,c) = (\sum c^\rho)^{1/\rho} = J^{1/\rho} c\). So \(\log(G/(J c)) = (1/\rho - 1)\log J = ((1-\rho)/\rho)\log J\). Using \((1-\rho)/\rho = KJ/((J-1)\rho)\) gives the result. 0◻

Proof of Theorem 7 (Geometric Unity)

The log-output Hessian is: \[\frac{\partial^2 \log F}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}\bigg|_{c\mathbf{1}} = -\frac{1-\rho}{Jc^2}\left(\delta_{ij} - \frac{1}{J}\right).\] So \(-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}\) has eigenvalue \((1-\rho)/(Jc^2) = K/((J-1)c^2)\) with multiplicity \(J-1\). This equals \([(1-\rho)/\rho^2] \cdot [\rho^2/(Jc^2)]\), which is the bridge ratio times the escort Fisher eigenvalue (see for the Fisher information computation). All four roles follow from powers of this single eigenvalue. 0◻

General Weights Extension

All main-text results assume symmetric CES. The general-weights extension \(F(x) = (\sum_j w_j x_j^\rho)^{1/\rho}\) with \(\sum w_j = 1\) modifies the results as follows. The eigenvalues of \(-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}\) are no longer degenerate: they are determined by the secular equation \[\det\left[\lambda I - \frac{1-\rho}{\rho} \text{diag}(w) + \frac{1-\rho}{\rho} w w^T\right] = 0,\] which yields \(J-1\) distinct eigenvalues. The bridge theorem, estimation paradox, and quadruple role all generalize, with \(K\) replaced by the spectral gap of the weighted Hessian.
