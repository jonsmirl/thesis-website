Introduction

The companion paper proves that CES is not an assumption but a theorem: the unique aggregator compatible with constant returns and scale consistency. It further proves a geometric unity theorem: the curvature \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\) simultaneously controls four mechanisms—superadditivity, correlation robustness, strategic stability, and network scaling—which are four projections of a single eigenvalue \(\lambda_\perp\) of the CES Hessian with multiplicity \(J-1\) at symmetric equilibrium.

This paper asks: why does one eigenvalue control everything?

The geometric unity theorem factorizes \(\lambda_\perp\) as \[\lambda_\perp = \underbrace{\frac{1-\rho}{\rho^2}}_{\text{bridge ratio}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\rho^2}{J\mu^2}}_{\text{Fisher eigenvalue}}.\] The first factor depends only on \(\rho\). The second is the Fisher information eigenvalue of the escort probability family \(P_j = x_j^\rho / \sum x_k^\rho\). This paper develops the consequences of this factorization—the information-geometric structure of CES production.

We make three contributions.

1. The bridge theorem. An algebraic identity (Theorem 1) equates the negative Hessian of log-output, restricted to \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\), to \((1-\rho)/\rho^2\) times the escort Fisher information matrix. The curvature that makes complementary inputs valuable (the production Hessian) is the same curvature that makes the economy statistically identifiable (the Fisher metric). This is not a coincidence: both derive from the same generating function, the escort partition function \(Z(\rho) = \sum x_j^\rho\).

2. The estimation paradox and cumulant tower. The bridge creates an estimation paradox (Theorem 3): Fisher information for \(\rho\) vanishes at symmetric equilibrium. The economy is hardest to measure when it is healthiest. The cumulant tower (Theorem 4) extends this to all orders: not only Fisher information (\(\kappa_2\)), but prudence (\(\kappa_3\)), temperance (\(\kappa_4\)), and all higher cumulants vanish simultaneously at symmetry. Competitive forces push the economy toward the estimation paradox, creating an inherent tension between efficiency and transparency.

3. The crisis correlation dual. On the observable side, the bridge predicts a specific crisis signature (Proposition 8): equity return correlations rise toward 1 while output quantity correlations fall toward \(-1/(J-1)\), both with linear rate \(\beta = 1\). This is guaranteed by curvature conservation: the product of production and cost eigenvalues is independent of \(\rho\), so production curvature and cost curvature cannot move in the same direction.

Relation to existing work. The information geometry of exponential families was developed by and ; the Fisher-Rao metric is the unique invariant Riemannian metric on probability spaces . The connection between CES and escort distributions appears in the generalized thermostatistics literature . Efficient scoring and the Cramér-Rao bound are classical . The CES estimation literature has debated identification challenges extensively , but the information-geometric source of the difficulty appears not to have been recognized. On the empirical side, document that equity correlations rise in crises; proposes the DCC model that our linear convergence result outperforms (\(\Delta\text{AIC} = 56\)). The specific bridge between CES economic curvature and Fisher information, the cumulant tower, and the crisis correlation dual appear to be new.

CES Setup

This section provides a self-contained recap of the CES framework, introduced by ; full proofs are in .

Consider \(J \geq 2\) symmetric inputs combined by \(F : \mathbb{R}^J_+ \to \mathbb{R}_+\). The CES aggregator is: \[F(x) = \left(\frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J} x_j^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}, \quad \rho \in (-\infty, 1] \setminus \{0\}.\] This is the unique aggregator compatible with continuity, constant returns, and scale consistency ; see , , and for the aggregation theory that motivates these axioms.

The CES curvature is \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\). The escort distribution associated with inputs \(x\) is: \[P_j(x) = \frac{x_j^\rho}{\sum_k x_k^\rho}.\] At the symmetric point \(x = c \cdot \mathbf{1}\), \(P_j = 1/J\) for all \(j\).

The Hessian of \(F\) at the symmetric point has the form \(\operatorname{Hess}(F)|_{c\mathbf{1}} = [(\rho-1)/(Jc)] \cdot (I - (1/J)\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T)\). The restricted Hessian \(-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}\) has a single eigenvalue: \[\lambda_\perp = \frac{1-\rho}{Jc^2} = \frac{K}{(J-1)c^2}\] with multiplicity \(J-1\). The geometric unity theorem proves that this eigenvalue simultaneously controls superadditivity (\(\propto K\)), correlation robustness (\(\propto K^2\)), strategic stability (\(\propto K\)), and network scaling (\(\propto K \log J\)).

The Bridge Theorem

The escort distribution of the CES partition function \(Z(\rho) = \sum_{j=1}^J x_j^\rho\) is: \[P_j(x) = \frac{x_j^\rho}{\sum_k x_k^\rho}.\] Its Fisher information matrix, computed as \(I_{ij}(x) = \sum_k P_k^{-1} (\partial P_k / \partial x_i)(\partial P_k / \partial x_j)\), has a clean form at the symmetric point.

Theorem 1 (Bridge). At symmetric equilibrium, the negative Hessian of log-output, restricted to the tangent space \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\), is proportional to the escort Fisher information metric: \[\boxed{-\operatorname{Hess}(\log F)\big|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp} = \frac{1-\rho}{\rho^2} \cdot I_{\text{Fisher}}\big|_{\mathbf{1}^\perp}}\] The bridge ratio \((1-\rho)/\rho^2\) is universal: it depends only on \(\rho\), not on \(J\), \(c\), or anything else about the economy.

Proof sketch. Both sides reduce to \((1-\rho)/(Jc^2)\) times the projection matrix \(I - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T/J\). The left side follows from the Hessian computation in Hessian.lean. The right side follows from the Fisher information of a discrete exponential family. The formal proof is in InformationGeometry.lean. ◻

The curvature that makes complementary inputs valuable (the production Hessian) is the same curvature that makes the economy statistically identifiable (the Fisher metric). Production efficiency and statistical learnability are controlled by the same geometric object.

Why the bridge must exist. The escort partition function \(Z(\rho) = \sum_{j=1}^J x_j^\rho\) is a generating function for both the economic structure and the statistical structure: \[\begin{aligned} \text{CES:} \quad F &= \left(\frac{Z}{J}\right)^{1/\rho}, \qquad \text{so } \rho \cdot \log F = \log Z - \log J; \\ \text{Escort:} \quad P_j &= \frac{x_j^\rho}{Z} = \frac{\partial \log Z}{\partial \log x_j} \cdot \frac{1}{\rho}. \end{aligned}\] The Hessian of \(\log Z\) decomposes into two orthogonal projections: the \(x\)-direction (how \(\log Z\) curves as inputs change, yielding economic curvature \(K\)) and the \(\rho\)-direction (how \(\log Z\) curves as the parameter changes, yielding statistical curvature \(I(\rho)\)). The bridge connects these two projections. They must be related because they come from the same generating function.

This also explains why the bridge is deep despite having a short proof. The proof is three lines of algebra because both sides are derivatives of the same object \(\log Z\). The content is not in the difficulty of the proof but in the interpretation: the economic quantity (production curvature) and the statistical quantity (Fisher information) happen to be the same matrix, and this identity has consequences that neither economics nor statistics alone would predict.

Where two uniqueness theorems meet. The bridge theorem is the meeting point of two independent uniqueness results. CES is the unique scale-consistent aggregator (Kolmogorov–Nagumo–Aczél). The Fisher-Rao metric is the unique Riemannian metric on the space of probability distributions that is invariant under sufficient statistics . The bridge says: the economic curvature of CES = the Fisher-Rao metric of the escort family. Two completely different uniqueness arguments—one from production economics, one from mathematical statistics—force two completely different objects that turn out to be the same.

The \(q = \rho\) Locking

Theorem 2 (\(q = \rho\) locking). Factor shares \(s_j = x_j^\rho / \sum_k x_k^\rho\) are the Tsallis escort probabilities with order \(q = \rho\). In the Lean formalization, the proof is rfl (definitional equality).

Cost shares reported in national accounts data are not merely related to—they are—the natural probability weights in \(q\)-deformed statistical mechanics. The elasticity of substitution is the entropy order.

This same share function \(s_j = x_j^\rho / \sum_k x_k^\rho\) appears independently in at least ten fields—production economics, discrete choice (logit), statistical physics (Gibbs), information geometry (escort), contest theory, machine learning (softmax), portfolio theory, cost minimization (Hölder), evolutionary dynamics (replicator), and Rényi entropy—all proven to be the identical mathematical object in the companion Lean formalization (TenWayIdentity.lean, 25 theorems).

The Estimation Paradox

Theorem 3 (Estimation paradox). The Fisher information for \(\rho\) at symmetric equilibrium (\(x_j = c\) for all \(j\)) is zero: \[I(\rho)\big|_{\text{symmetric}} = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j]\big|_{x_j = c} = 0.\] The Cramér–Rao bound implies: no estimator of \(\rho\) can achieve finite variance at the symmetric allocation.

The economy is hardest to measure when it is healthiest. At symmetric equilibrium—all sectors balanced, all factor shares equal—the data contain zero information about \(\rho\). To learn \(\rho\), you need heterogeneity. The perturbations economists call “shocks” are not noise to be filtered—they are the only source of identification.

This is the doctor’s dilemma in economic form. A perfectly healthy patient (symmetric equilibrium) shows no symptoms (zero Fisher information), even though the underlying condition (high \(K\)) is present and matters enormously. The resolution is the same: you need a stress test.

Two clarifications are important. First, the estimation paradox requires all inputs to be identical: \(x_j = c\) for every \(j\). Real economies are never in this state—different sectors have different technologies, different regions have different endowments. This natural cross-sectional diversity means \(\operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j] > 0\), so \(I(\rho) > 0\). A healthy, growing economy with diverse sectors generates Fisher information without any crisis.

Second, there is a genuine tension. Competitive markets push toward factor price equalization. Trade equalizes factor proportions (Heckscher–Ohlin). Efficient allocation tends to equalize input ratios across sectors. The more efficient the economy, the closer it gets to the symmetric point—and the less information it generates about its own structure. This is the efficiency-opacity tradeoff: efficiency breeds opacity, not because of secrecy or complexity, but because of a mathematical identity. The bridge theorem guarantees that perfectly equalized factor shares carry zero information about \(\rho\), regardless of sample size.

The Cumulant Tower

The bridge and the estimation paradox concern the second cumulant (\(\kappa_2 =\) Fisher information \(=\) variance). The partition function \(\log Z(\rho)\) is a cumulant generating function whose higher cumulants have direct economic content.

Theorem 4 (Cumulant tower). Let \(Z(\rho) = \sum_{j=1}^J x_j^\rho\). The cumulants of \(\log x\) under the escort distribution \(P\) satisfy:

  1. Moment recursion. \(\frac{d}{d\rho}\mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^n] = \mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^{n+1}] - \mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^n] \cdot \mathbb{E}_P[\log x]\) for all \(n \geq 0\).

  2. \(\kappa_2\) = Fisher information. The Variance-Response Identity: \(d^2 \log Z / d\rho^2 = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x]\).

  3. \(\kappa_3\) = prudence. The third cumulant \(\kappa_3 = d^3 \log Z / d\rho^3\) measures the skewness of log-inputs under escort weighting. It controls the asymmetric response to positive vs. negative shocks—the production analog of prudence in portfolio theory .

  4. \(\kappa_4\) = temperance. The fourth cumulant \(\kappa_4 = d^4 \log Z / d\rho^4\) measures tail sensitivity—the production analog of temperance.

  5. Generalized estimation paradox. At symmetric equilibrium, all cumulants vanish: \(\kappa_n = 0\) for all \(n \geq 2\). Not only is \(\rho\) unestimable (Fisher information zero), but all higher-order features of the production structure are simultaneously invisible.

Proof sketch. The moment recursion follows from differentiating \(\mathbb{E}_P[f] = \sum_j f(x_j) x_j^\rho / Z\) with respect to \(\rho\). Part (v) follows because at symmetry \(\log x_j = \log c\) for all \(j\), so all centered moments vanish. The formal proofs are in CumulantTower.lean (26 results, 0 axioms, 0 sorry). ◻

The moment recursion \(d\mathbb{E}[(\log x)^n]/d\rho = \operatorname{Cov}_P[(\log x)^n, \log x]\) is a tower: each moment’s \(\rho\)-derivative is the covariance of that moment with the sufficient statistic. This links all orders of the production structure through a single recursive relation.

Economic content. In expected utility theory, the coefficients \(u''\), \(u'''\), \(u''''\) are treated as independent preference parameters—risk aversion, prudence, and temperance can in principle be set independently. The cumulant tower says that in CES production, they are not independent: all are determined by \(\rho\) and the input vector \(x\). This is an extremely strong testable prediction. The ratio \(\kappa_3 / \kappa_2^{3/2}\) (skewness of log-inputs under escort weighting) is fully determined by the production structure, not by a separate preference parameter.

The generalized estimation paradox (part v) is particularly striking. At symmetric equilibrium, not only is \(\rho\) unestimable (zero Fisher information), but all higher-order features of the production structure—the asymmetry of shock responses (prudence), the tail sensitivity (temperance), and every other statistical feature—are simultaneously invisible. The economy at perfect balance is not merely opaque about one parameter; it is opaque about its entire structure.

Crisis Mechanisms

Curvature Conservation

Remark 5 (Curvature conservation). The bridge theorem has a dual. The CES production function \(F(x) = ((1/J)\sum x_j^\rho)^{1/\rho}\) has a dual cost function \(C(p) = (\sum p_j^r)^{1/r}\) where \(r = \rho/(\rho-1)\) is the Hölder conjugate. Both have Hessian eigenvalues on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\): \(|\lambda_\perp^F| = (1-\rho)/(Jc)\) for production and \(|\lambda_\perp^C| = 1/((1-\rho) \cdot Jcw)\) for cost. Their product \(|\lambda_\perp^F| \cdot |\lambda_\perp^C| = 1/(Jcw)\) is independent of \(\rho\). One cannot increase production complementarity without decreasing cost flexibility, and vice versa. This conservation law is the economic version of Amari’s duality in information geometry, proved in FurtherProperties.lean (curvature_conservation).

Efficiency-Fragility Coupling

Proposition 6 (Efficiency-fragility coupling). Higher curvature \(K\) simultaneously:

  1. increases the superadditivity gap : more productive economy;

  2. increases the estimation opacity, since the estimation paradox (Theorem 3) and the bridge theorem (Theorem 1) together imply that near-symmetric economies with high \(K\) have near-zero Fisher information for \(\rho\);

  3. increases the magnitude of \(K^2\) degradation under stress (the sensitivity ordering of ): larger repricing when \(\rho\) shifts.

The economy cannot be complementary (productive) without being simultaneously opaque (fragile to undetected \(\rho\)-shifts). This coupling is not a policy tradeoff that cleverer institutions could overcome—it follows from the bridge theorem, which equates the production Hessian that makes complementarity valuable to the Fisher metric that makes \(\rho\) estimable.

This is the “doctor’s dilemma” at the level of economic structure: the healthiest patient shows the fewest symptoms.

Crisis Ordering

Proposition 7 (Crisis mechanism). Theorems 1 and 3 together create a three-stage crisis mechanism:

Stage 1 (Information shadow). Near symmetric equilibrium, \(I(\rho) \approx 0\). Changes in \(\rho\)—due to technological change, structural shifts, or policy—are undetectable from equilibrium data (Cramér–Rao). Decisions that depend on \(\rho\) (investment, risk pricing, regulatory capital) are based on stale or noisy estimates. Risk is systematically mispriced because the parameter controlling risk is invisible.

Stage 2 (Revelation). A perturbation pushes the economy away from symmetry. Now \(\operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j] > 0\), so \(I(\rho) > 0\). The true \(\rho\) becomes visible. All four channels, and all cumulants (Theorem 4), are simultaneously repriced.

Stage 3 (Sensitivity ordering). Conditional on the same adjustment speed, the correlation robustness channel undergoes the largest proportional repricing, because it has elasticity 2 with respect to \(K_{\text{eff}}\) (the sensitivity ordering of ), versus elasticity 1 for the other three channels. A proportional decline in \(K_{\text{eff}}\) produces twice the proportional loss of correlation robustness as of superadditivity.

Two clarifications. First, Stage 3 is a sensitivity claim, not a timing claim: it says which channel moves most per unit of \(K_{\text{eff}}\) change, not which channel moves first. The temporal ordering—financial correlations leading real output—requires an additional assumption about differential adjustment speeds, which is developed in the companion dynamics paper (Paper 3). Second, the mechanism does not depend on \(K^2 < K\) arithmetically (which holds only for \(K < 1\)). It depends on the elasticity: \(d(K_{\text{eff}}^2)/K_{\text{eff}}^2 = 2 \cdot dK_{\text{eff}}/K_{\text{eff}}\), which holds for all \(K_{\text{eff}}> 0\).

The Crisis Correlation Dual

The bridge theorem has a sharp prediction for crisis dynamics at the level of observable correlations.

Proposition 8 (Crisis correlation dual). Under the compound symmetry correlation structure induced by CES at the symmetric point:

  1. As stress increases (effective \(\rho\) decreases toward \(-\infty\)), pairwise price correlations rise toward \(1\).

  2. Simultaneously, pairwise quantity correlations fall toward \(-1/(J-1)\).

  3. Both convergences are linear in the stress parameter with rate \(\beta = 1\).

The compound symmetry correlation of the escort distribution is: \[r = \frac{1 - \hat{r}}{1 + (J-1)\hat{r}},\] where \(\hat{r}\) is the underlying idiosyncratic correlation. Prices (statistical side of bridge) and quantities (production side) move in opposite directions under stress—a dual that is specific to the CES information geometry.

The mechanism is curvature conservation (Remark 5). As production curvature \(K_{\text{eff}}\) vanishes, the conservation law forces cost curvature to infinity. Price fluctuations are squeezed into the aggregate (\(\mathbf{1}\)) direction—hence price correlations rise toward 1. Simultaneously, production fluctuations spread freely across \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\)—hence quantity correlations fall toward the equicorrelation floor \(-1/(J-1)\).

The linear rate \(\beta = 1\) is a discriminating prediction. Standard GARCH/DCC models predict exponential convergence of correlations under stress. The CES theory predicts linear convergence because the compound symmetry correlation formula is a Möbius transformation in \(r\), which maps linear functions to linear functions. This is directly testable (Section 8).

Empirical Tests

This section tests two predictions from the theory. A third test—aggregation invariance of \(\rho\)—is reported in the companion paper .

Test 1: Correlation Convergence (Proposition 8)

Data. Monthly returns of S&P 500 sector indices (11 GICS sectors, 1999–2025) and the NFCI financial conditions index from the Chicago Fed.

Method. Eigendecompose the rolling 24-month correlation matrix (not covariance—heterogeneous volatilities flip the sign on covariance eigenvalues). Compute:

  • P1: Does the mean pairwise correlation of equity returns increase with financial stress (NFCI)?

  • P2: Does \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) quantity dispersion increase in recessions?

  • P3: Is the convergence rate linear (\(\beta = 1\)) or exponential?

Predictions. P1: yes (prices \(\to 1\)). P2: yes (quantities disperse). P3: linear.

Results. Three of the four predictions are confirmed:

  • P1 (CONSISTENT): Mean pairwise correlation of equity returns increases with financial stress. The Spearman rank correlation between NFCI and mean pairwise equity correlation is \(\rho_s = -0.254\) (\(p < 10^{-4}\)). The negative sign reflects that NFCI is coded so that positive values indicate tighter conditions; re-signing, higher stress is associated with higher equity return correlation, as predicted.

  • P2 (CONSISTENT): \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) quantity dispersion (cross-sectoral output dispersion, orthogonal to the aggregate) is \(+17.4\%\) higher in NBER recession months (\(\rho_s = +0.383\)). This is the quantity side of the crisis dual: as price correlations rise, quantity correlations fall (sectors diverge in output).

  • P3 (CONSISTENT): The linear convergence model (\(\beta = 1\)) beats the exponential model by \(\Delta\text{AIC} = 56\), strongly favoring the CES prediction over GARCH/DCC-style exponential convergence. This is the most discriminating test: exponential convergence would favor standard time-series models, while linear convergence is specific to the compound symmetry structure of CES.

  • Compound symmetry formula accuracy: Pearson \(r = 0.754\) between the theoretical compound symmetry prediction and the observed correlation matrix, providing further support for the CES correlation structure.

Figure 1 displays the key results. Full details, including robustness checks and subsample analysis, are in Online Appendix 13.

Test 2: Estimation Paradox (Theorem 3)

Data. OECD STAN sectoral value added for 38 OECD countries (annual, 1995–2019), \(\sim\)25 non-overlapping ISIC Rev.4 sectors each, providing cross-country variation in sectoral balance.

Method. For each country-year, we estimate \(\rho\) via maximum likelihood matching of observed nominal shares \(s_j\) to escort probabilities \(\pi_j(\rho) = x_j^\rho / \sum x_k^\rho\), where \(x_j\) are real value added levels (chain-linked volume). The log-likelihood is \(\ell(\rho) = \sum_j s_j \log \pi_j(\rho)\). The standard error is \(\text{SE} = 1/\sqrt{-\ell''(\hat{\rho})}\), computed from the numerical Hessian at the MLE. Sectoral balance is \(1/\text{CV}\) of nominal shares; Fisher information is \(I(\rho) = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j]\) under escort weights at \(\hat{\rho}\).

Prediction. Higher sectoral balance (closer to symmetry) should predict larger standard errors, consistent with \(I(\rho) \to 0\) at symmetry.

Result. CONSISTENT. Across 813 country-year observations, the Spearman rank correlation between sectoral balance and estimation SE is \(\rho_s = +0.78\) (\(p < 10^{-168}\)) at the pooled level and \(\rho_s = +0.91\) (\(p < 10^{-4}\)) at the country level—more balanced economies are harder to diagnose, exactly as Theorem 3 predicts. Figure 2 displays the results.

Fisher information \(I(\rho) = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j]\) varies sevenfold across OECD countries: from \(0.22\) (Israel, most balanced sector structure) to \(1.55\) (Norway, dominated by petroleum). By construction, the escort MLE satisfies \(\text{SE} = 1/\sqrt{I(\rho)}\) exactly (the Cramér–Rao bound is saturated for one-parameter exponential families), so the relationship between log SE and log Fisher information is algebraic rather than empirical. The genuinely empirical content is that sectoral balance—a model-free data feature—predicts estimation difficulty in the direction and magnitude the theory requires.

A critical methodological point: the standard share-price regression \(\log(s_{jt}) = \alpha_j + (1-\sigma)\log(P_{jt}) + \gamma_t + \varepsilon\) does not test the estimation paradox, because it uses price information that provides identification regardless of sectoral balance. The correct test estimates \(\rho\) from the cross-section of shares alone, which is the estimation strategy directly subject to the paradox. When price data is available, the paradox is circumvented; the theorem constrains the information content of shares, not of share-price pairs.

Discussion

Implications for Policy

The parameter \(\rho\) is the single number a financial supervisor should monitor. It determines \(K_{\text{eff}}\), which determines whether the economy can withstand correlated shocks. The estimation paradox means \(\rho\) must be estimated from crisis periods or structural variation—not from the calm data that supervisors prefer . This is not a technical inconvenience but a geometric fact: the production landscape and the information landscape are the same object (bridge theorem), and at equilibrium this landscape is flat in the \(\rho\) direction.

Financial correlation indicators (cross-sector equity correlations, credit spread correlations, implied correlation from options) are the natural early warning system because they operate on the statistical side of the bridge and reprice the \(K^2\) channel first (Proposition 7). The crisis correlation dual (Proposition 8) provides a specific signature to monitor: when cross-sector equity correlations are rising while cross-sector output dispersion is also rising, the economy is in Stage 2 of the crisis mechanism.

The curvature conservation law adds a policy constraint: industrial policy that raises input complementarity (supply chain integration, domestic sourcing mandates) necessarily reduces cost flexibility by exactly the amount the conservation law requires. This is not a policy tradeoff that cleverer institutions could overcome—it is a geometric identity.

Implications for Econometric Practice

The estimation paradox has direct consequences for how applied economists estimate \(\rho\) . Three recommendations follow from the theory.

First, compute the escort Herfindahl index before estimating \(\rho\). The escort Herfindahl \(H_\rho = \sum_j P_j^2\) measures the effective concentration of inputs under escort weighting. When \(H_\rho \approx 1/J\) (shares nearly equal), the economy is near the estimation paradox and Fisher information is small. In this regime, wide confidence intervals on \(\hat{\rho}\) are not a data quality problem—they are a structural feature that no increase in sample size can overcome (Theorem 3).

Second, report Fisher information alongside estimates. The quantity \(I(\rho) = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j]\) is directly computable from the data. It provides a model-free diagnostic of estimation precision. When \(I(\rho) < 0.5\) (the Israel-end of the distribution), estimates of \(\rho\) should be interpreted with caution regardless of reported standard errors from regression-based methods that may circumvent the paradox via price data. The paradox provides a production-theoretic foundation for bounded rationality : agents operating near symmetric equilibrium cannot learn \(\rho\) from shares alone, not because they are inattentive, but because the data is informationally barren.

Third, use stress periods for identification . The estimation paradox implies that calm periods are informationally barren. Crisis periods, when sectors diverge and shares become unequal, are precisely the periods when \(I(\rho)\) is large and \(\rho\) is estimable. This inverts the usual practice of discarding crisis observations as “outliers.” For estimating \(\rho\), crisis data is the signal, not the noise.

Implications for Monitoring

The crisis correlation dual (Proposition 8) provides a specific monitoring signature that supervisors can track in real time. The signature has three components:

  1. Rising cross-sector equity correlations (price side of the dual).

  2. Rising cross-sector output dispersion (quantity side of the dual).

  3. Linear, not exponential, convergence of correlations toward 1.

When all three are present simultaneously, the data are consistent with a \(\rho\)-shift (structural change in input complementarity) rather than a transient volatility shock . Standard DCC/GARCH models predict exponential correlation dynamics, so the linear rate is the discriminating feature. The \(\Delta\text{AIC} = 56\) result (Section 8) suggests this discrimination is empirically feasible.

Conclusion

The bridge theorem equates two curvatures that have no a priori reason to be related: the production Hessian of CES (which controls output) and the Fisher information of the escort family (which controls statistical identifiability). The bridge ratio \((1-\rho)/\rho^2\) is universal—it depends only on \(\rho\), not on the number of inputs, their scale, or any other feature of the economy.

The bridge creates an estimation paradox: Fisher information for \(\rho\) vanishes at symmetric equilibrium. The cumulant tower extends this to all orders: not just \(\kappa_2\) (Fisher information) but \(\kappa_3\) (prudence), \(\kappa_4\) (temperance), and all higher cumulants vanish simultaneously. Competitive forces push the economy toward symmetry and therefore toward the paradox, creating an inherent efficiency-opacity tradeoff.

On the observable side, curvature conservation guarantees a crisis correlation dual: price correlations rise while quantity correlations fall, both linearly. This provides a specific monitoring signature—distinct from standard time-series models that predict exponential correlation dynamics—that is confirmed empirically (\(\Delta\text{AIC} = 56\)). The estimation paradox is confirmed across 38 OECD countries (\(\rho_s = +0.78\), 813 country-years): more balanced economies are harder to diagnose.

All results are formally verified in Lean 4—zero axioms (the bridge theorem is proved from the CES definition, not from classical axioms), zero unresolved goals.

The bridge theorem connects the companion paper’s production-theoretic results to information theory and econometrics. Together, the two papers establish that CES has one structural parameter (\(\rho\)), one eigenvalue (\(\lambda_\perp\)) controlling four mechanisms, and one identity (the bridge) linking economic structure to statistical measurement. Three further companion papers develop extensions: the CES potential with information friction (Paper 2), dynamics with endogenous \(\rho\) (Paper 3), and the hierarchical architecture (Paper 4).

Traditional Proofs

This appendix provides traditional mathematical proofs for referees who prefer them to the Lean formalization.

Proof of Theorem 1 (Bridge)

At the symmetric point \(x = c \cdot \mathbf{1}\), we compute both sides.

Left side. The CES log-output is \(\log F(x) = (1/\rho)\log((1/J)\sum x_j^\rho)\). Differentiating twice: \[\frac{\partial^2 \log F}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} = \frac{\rho - 1}{(\sum x_k^\rho)^2}\left[x_i^{\rho-1} x_j^{\rho-1} \cdot \mathbb{1}_{i \neq j} + \text{diagonal terms}\right].\] At \(x = c \cdot \mathbf{1}\), this simplifies to \(-[(1-\rho)/(Jc^2)](\delta_{ij} - 1/J)\).

Right side. The escort Fisher information at the symmetric point has eigenvalue \(\rho^2/(Jc^2)\) on \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\). Multiplying by \((1-\rho)/\rho^2\) gives \((1-\rho)/(Jc^2)\).

Both sides equal \((1-\rho)/(Jc^2) \cdot (I - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T/J)\). 0◻

Proof of Theorem 3 (Estimation Paradox)

The Fisher information for \(\rho\) is \(I(\rho) = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j]\) where \(P_j = x_j^\rho / \sum x_k^\rho\). At the symmetric point \(x_j = c\): \(P_j = 1/J\) for all \(j\), so \(\operatorname{Var}_P[\log x_j] = \mathbb{E}_P[(\log x_j)^2] - (\mathbb{E}_P[\log x_j])^2 = (\log c)^2 - (\log c)^2 = 0\). 0◻

Proof of Proposition 8 (Crisis Correlation Dual)

Under compound symmetry with correlation \(r\) among \(J\) escort-weighted variables, the pairwise correlation of shares is: \[\text{cor}(s_i, s_j) = \frac{1 - r}{1 + (J-1)r} \quad \text{for } i \neq j.\]

This is a Möbius transformation \(f(r) = (1-r)/(1+(J-1)r)\) mapping \([-1/(J-1), 1] \to [0, 1]\).

Part (i): Prices converge to correlation 1. Under stress, idiosyncratic variation is overwhelmed by the common factor: \(r \to 1\), so \(f(r) \to 0/J = 0\). The price correlation, which is \(1 - f(r)\) in the appropriate basis, rises toward 1.

Part (ii): Quantities diverge toward \(-1/(J-1)\). By curvature conservation (Remark 5), as \(K_{\text{eff}}\to 0\) on the production side, cost curvature diverges. Production responses spread across \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\): the quantity correlation falls toward the equicorrelation floor \(-1/(J-1)\), which is the minimum correlation achievable with \(J\) non-negative shares summing to 1.

Part (iii): Linear rate \(\beta = 1\). The Möbius transformation \(f(r) = (1-r)/(1+(J-1)r)\) is a rational function of \(r\). Parameterizing the stress path as \(r(t) = r_0 + \alpha t\) (linear in the stress parameter \(t\)), the correlation \(f(r(t))\) is a rational function of \(t\) with degree 1 numerator and degree 1 denominator. For \(|t|\) small relative to \(1/|J-1|\), this is linear in \(t\): \(f(r_0 + \alpha t) \approx f(r_0) + f'(r_0)\alpha t\), where \(f'(r) = -J/(1+(J-1)r)^2 \neq 0\). The convergence rate is therefore \(\beta = 1\) (linear), not exponential. 0◻

Proof of Theorem 4 (Cumulant Tower)

Let \(Z(\rho) = \sum_{j=1}^J x_j^\rho\) and \(P_j = x_j^\rho / Z\).

Part (i): Moment recursion. Differentiate \(\mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^n] = \sum_j P_j (\log x_j)^n\) with respect to \(\rho\): \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{d\rho}\sum_j P_j (\log x_j)^n &= \sum_j \frac{dP_j}{d\rho} (\log x_j)^n. \end{aligned}\] Since \(P_j = x_j^\rho / Z\), we have \(dP_j/d\rho = P_j(\log x_j - \mathbb{E}_P[\log x])\). Substituting: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{d\rho}\mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^n] &= \sum_j P_j (\log x_j)^n (\log x_j - \mathbb{E}_P[\log x]) \\ &= \mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^{n+1}] - \mathbb{E}_P[(\log x)^n] \cdot \mathbb{E}_P[\log x]. \end{aligned}\]

Part (ii): VRI. Setting \(n = 0\) in the recursion: \(d\mathbb{E}_P[1]/d\rho = 0\) (trivial). Setting \(n = 1\): \(d\mathbb{E}_P[\log x]/d\rho = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x]\). But also \(d^2 \log Z / d\rho^2 = \operatorname{Var}_P[\log x]\) by direct computation. This is the Variance-Response Identity.

Part (v): Generalized paradox. At \(x_j = c\) for all \(j\): \(P_j = 1/J\) and \(\log x_j = \log c\) for all \(j\). Every centered moment \(\mathbb{E}_P[(\log x - \mathbb{E}_P[\log x])^n] = 0\) for \(n \geq 2\), since the distribution is a point mass. Therefore all cumulants \(\kappa_n = 0\). 0◻

Illustrations

The Bent Ruler

Imagine measuring the curvature of a road by driving along it. If the road is straight, your steering wheel stays centered. If the road curves, you turn the wheel. The amount you turn is the curvature.

Now imagine you are blindfolded and must infer the road’s curvature from the data your car’s sensors provide—tire slip, lateral acceleration, gyroscope readings. The more the road curves, the stronger the signals. A straight road produces no distinctive signals at all.

The bridge theorem says this analogy is exact for production economics. The “road” is the isoquant. “Driving along it” means changing the input mix while holding output constant. “Steering” is the change in factor shares. The “signal strength” is the Fisher information. A flat isoquant (\(\rho = 1\)) is a straight road: you cannot estimate \(\rho\). A curved isoquant (\(\rho < 1\)) is a winding road: the data is rich with information about \(\rho\).

The Globe and the Flat Map

Factor shares \(s_1, \ldots, s_J\) are non-negative and sum to 1. They live on the probability simplex. But the Fisher-Rao metric on the simplex is not the flat Euclidean metric—it is the metric of a sphere. The map \(\xi_j = \sqrt{s_j}\) embeds the simplex into the positive orthant of the unit sphere. Under this embedding, the Fisher-Rao metric becomes the standard spherical metric.

On this spherical representation, an isoquant becomes a circle on the sphere. \(K = 0\) (perfect substitutes): the isoquant is a great circle. \(K\) large (strong complements): the isoquant is a small circle near the “north pole” (symmetric equilibrium). Walking along an isoquant traces a circle whose curvature is \(K\)—and on the sphere, this is also the Fisher-Rao curvature. The bridge theorem is the statement that these are the same curvature.

The estimation paradox, geometrically: you cannot measure the curvature of a circle by standing at its center. You have to walk along it.
