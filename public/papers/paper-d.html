Introduction

On August 9, 2007, BNP Paribas froze three investment funds, citing “a complete evaporation of liquidity.” The announcement surprised markets. The VIX had been trading below 12 for most of the preceding year—the lowest sustained level in the index’s history. Corporate bond spreads were tight. GDP growth was solid. By every standard indicator, the economy was healthy.

Yet the data told a different story if one knew where to look. Housing return correlations across U.S. metropolitan areas had begun spiking in mid-2006, a full 2.5 years before the NBER-dated recession trough. The ABX indices—synthetic credit default swaps on subprime mortgage-backed securities—had started declining in January 2007, eight months before BNP Paribas. BAA corporate bond spreads crossed two standard deviations above their mean in December 2007, three months before the Bear Stearns collapse. Industrial production did not begin its sustained decline until February 2008. The TED spread—measuring interbank coordination breakdown—did not exceed 200 basis points until September 2008, a full two years after housing correlations first signaled distress.

This is not a story about one crisis. The same sequence—correlations first, then output, then coordination—appears in the Asian financial crisis (1997), the dot-com bust (2001), the European sovereign debt crisis (2010), and, with compressed timing, the COVID shock (2020). The question is: why?

This paper derives the crisis sequence from the mathematics of CES production. The answer is that correlations, output, and coordination are controlled by different powers of a single curvature parameter \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\), and higher powers degrade faster as friction rises toward its critical threshold. The same parameter that makes complementary production valuable—superadditivity proportional to \(K\)—also makes the economy fragile to friction shocks, because the critical friction threshold \(T^*\) at which effective curvature collapses to zero is inversely proportional to \(K\). This is the Smirl Curve: the economy cannot be productive without being fragile, and the coupling is an algebraic identity, not a policy failure.

Three contributions. First, we derive the Crisis Sequence Theorem (Section 3): the three channels controlled by CES curvature degrade in strict order \(K^2 \to K \to K/J\) as information friction \(T\) rises toward \(T^*\). This ordering is not assumed but follows from the elasticities of each channel with respect to effective curvature \(K_{\text{eff}}= K(1 - T/T^*)^+\). The theorem generates the specific prediction that financial correlations spike before output declines, and output declines before coordination breaks down—precisely the GFC timeline.

Second, we formalize the Minsky Trap (Section 5): during booms, four identified channels drive \(\rho\) downward (increasing complementarity), which simultaneously raises productivity and lowers \(T^*\). The stability margin \(\Delta = T^*- T\) narrows endogenously even when the friction environment is stable. The system self-organizes toward its own critical threshold. The speed asymmetry is 8.5:1—compression during booms takes years; collapse during crises takes months.

Third, we establish the Smirl Curve (Section 4) as a fundamental constraint: the locus of \((\rho, T^*)\) pairs defines an impassable frontier. No institutional arrangement, no regulatory regime, no monetary policy can simultaneously achieve high complementarity (high \(K\), high output) and high friction tolerance (high \(T^*\)). The tradeoff is geometric, not institutional.

Empirical validation. Section 7 reports 19 consistent empirical tests. The stability-margin indicator \(\hat{\Delta}\) outperforms VIX at forecasting recessions. CPI component dispersion leads aggregate inflation at 18 of 18 turning points. Sectors ordered by estimated \(\rho\) fail first and recover last. The Minsky \(\rho\)-shift hypothesis holds with \(r = -0.886\) over the business cycle. Pre-crisis calm (low VIX) predicts crisis severity, consistent with the estimation paradox: the economy is hardest to measure when it is healthiest.

Relation to existing work. Minsky’s (1986) Financial Instability Hypothesis argues that stability breeds instability. We provide the missing mathematical mechanism: stability breeds complementarity, which lowers the friction threshold, which breeds instability. The “Minsky moment” is not metaphorical but algebraic—it occurs when \(T\) crosses \(T^*(\rho(t))\), where \(\rho(t)\) has been endogenously falling. The econometrics of early warning indicators identifies correlates of crises; the crisis sequence theorem explains why those correlates appear in the order they do. The DSGE literature following and treats \(\rho\) (or its equivalent) as a fixed structural parameter; we show \(\rho\) is endogenous to the business cycle, and that this endogeneity is essential for understanding crisis dynamics.

The CES Potential Landscape

Setup and the CES Aggregate

Consider \(J \geq 2\) sectors combined by the symmetric CES aggregator: \[\label{eq:ces} F(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J} x_j^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}, \quad \rho \in (-\infty, 1] \setminus \{0\},\] with the Cobb–Douglas limit \(F = (\prod x_j)^{1/J}\) at \(\rho = 0\). CES is not an assumption: it is the unique aggregator compatible with constant returns, continuity, and scale consistency (Kolmogorov 1930; Nagumo 1930; Aczél 1948). See Paper 1 for the formal proof and its Lean 4 verification.

The CES curvature is: \[\label{eq:K} K = \frac{(1-\rho)(J-1)}{J}.\] For complementary inputs (\(\rho < 1\)), \(K > 0\). For perfect substitutes (\(\rho = 1\)), \(K = 0\).

The CES Potential

Define the CES potential: \[\label{eq:potential} \Phi_q(\mathbf{x}; T) = \Phi_{\text{CES}}(\mathbf{x}; \rho) - T \cdot S_q(\mathbf{x}),\] where \(\Phi_{\text{CES}} = -\sum_{n} \log F_n\) is the aggregate CES welfare loss function, \(S_q\) is the Tsallis entropy of order \(q\), and \(T \geq 0\) is the information friction parameter measuring the costliness of transmitting allocation signals across agents and sectors.

The emergence theorem forces \(q = \rho\): the entropy order must equal the CES parameter, because both are determined by the same generating function \(Z(\rho) = \sum_j x_j^\rho\) (Paper 2, Theorem 1). Factor shares \(s_j = x_j^\rho / \sum_k x_k^\rho\) are simultaneously CES cost shares, Tsallis escort probabilities, logit choice probabilities, and Gibbs weights—all with the same parameter \(\rho\).

Effective Curvature and the Critical Threshold

Definition 1 (Effective curvature). The effective curvature under information friction \(T\) is: \[\label{eq:keff} K_{\text{eff}}(T) = K \cdot \left(1 - \frac{T}{T^*}\right)^+,\] where \((x)^+ = \max(x, 0)\) and \(T^*\) is the critical friction threshold.

Proposition 2 (Critical threshold). At symmetric equilibrium with \(J\) sectors and curvature parameter \(\rho < 1\): \[\label{eq:tstar} T^*(\rho, J) = \frac{cJ}{(1-\rho)(J-1)},\] where \(c > 0\) is the common input level. For \(T < T^*\), the symmetric equilibrium is a local minimum of \(\Phi_q\) (stable). For \(T > T^*\), the Hessian of \(\Phi_q\) acquires a negative eigenvalue (unstable). At \(T = T^*\), the Hessian is degenerate: the economy is at its critical point.

Proof sketch. The Hessian of \(\Phi_q\) at the symmetric point has eigenvalue \(\lambda_\perp(T) = (1-\rho)/(\rho J c^2) - T \cdot h(\rho)\) in the \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\) subspace, where \(h(\rho)\) is the second derivative of Tsallis entropy. Setting \(\lambda_\perp = 0\) and solving for \(T\) yields ([eq:tstar]). The formal proof is in Paper2/EffectiveCurvature.lean. ◻

The threshold \(T^*\) is where complementary production becomes unsustainable: friction costs exceed the curvature gains from input complementarity. Below \(T^*\), the economy sits in a basin of attraction around the efficient symmetric allocation. Above \(T^*\), the basin vanishes and the economy transitions to a corner solution (concentration in a few sectors).

The \((\rho, T)\) Regime Diagram

The plane \((\rho, T)\) divides into three regimes:

  1. Complementary regime (\(T < T^*\), \(\rho < 1\)): \(K_{\text{eff}}> 0\), symmetric equilibrium stable, diversified production efficient.

  2. Substitute regime (\(\rho > 1\) or \(T > T^*\)): effective curvature zero or negative, concentration efficient.

  3. Critical boundary (\(T = T^*\)): \(K_{\text{eff}}= 0\), pre-crisis deceleration, divergent variance.

The boundary curve \(T = T^*(\rho)\) is a hyperbola in the \((\rho, T)\) plane. This curve is the Smirl Curve, developed in Section 4.

The Crisis Sequence Theorem

Three Channels of CES Curvature

CES curvature controls three economic mechanisms (Paper 1, Theorem 2):

  1. Correlation robustness (\(\propto K^2\)): CES nonlinearity extracts idiosyncratic variation that linear aggregates miss. The informational bonus scales as \(\lambda_\perp^2 \propto K^2\).

  2. Superadditivity (\(\propto K\)): complementary inputs produce more together than the sum of parts. The gap is \(v^T \operatorname{Hess}(F) v = -\lambda_\perp \|v\|^2 \propto K\).

  3. Coordination premium (\(\propto K/J\)): the per-sector gain from Nash coordination at balanced equilibrium. Each sector’s share of the total curvature benefit is \(K/J\).

Under friction \(T\), each channel operates at its effective level, obtained by substituting \(K_{\text{eff}}\) for \(K\): \[\begin{aligned} \text{Correlation robustness:} \quad & R(T) = \alpha_R \cdot K_{\text{eff}}^2(T), \label{eq:corr} \\ \text{Superadditivity:} \quad & A(T) = \alpha_A \cdot K_{\text{eff}}(T), \label{eq:super} \\ \text{Coordination:} \quad & C(T) = \alpha_C \cdot K_{\text{eff}}(T)/J, \label{eq:coord} \end{aligned}\] where \(\alpha_R, \alpha_A, \alpha_C > 0\) are structural constants independent of \(T\).

The Crisis Sequence

Theorem 3 (Crisis sequence). As information friction \(T\) rises from \(0\) toward \(T^*\), the three channels degrade in strict order: \[\label{eq:sequence} K^2 \;\longrightarrow\; K \;\longrightarrow\; K/J.\] Specifically, define the fractional loss of channel \(X\) as \(\ell_X(T) = 1 - X(T)/X(0)\). Then for all \(T \in (0, T^*)\):

  1. \(\ell_R(T) > \ell_A(T) > \ell_C(T)\) for all \(T > 0\).

  2. The elasticity of each channel with respect to \(K_{\text{eff}}\) determines the ordering: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \log R}{\partial \log K_{\text{eff}}} &= 2, \label{eq:elast_corr} \\ \frac{\partial \log A}{\partial \log K_{\text{eff}}} &= 1, \label{eq:elast_super} \\ \frac{\partial \log C}{\partial \log K_{\text{eff}}} &= 1. \label{eq:elast_coord} \end{aligned}\]

  3. For a proportional decline \(K_{\text{eff}}\to (1-\epsilon)K_{\text{eff}}\), the fractional losses are: \[\begin{aligned} \ell_R &= 1 - (1-\epsilon)^2 \approx 2\epsilon, \\ \ell_A &= 1 - (1-\epsilon) = \epsilon, \\ \ell_C &= 1 - (1-\epsilon) = \epsilon. \end{aligned}\] Correlation robustness loses twice as much as superadditivity for the same \(K_{\text{eff}}\) decline.

Proof. The proof is direct. Since \(R \propto K_{\text{eff}}^2\) and \(A \propto K_{\text{eff}}\), we have \(R(T)/R(0) = [K_{\text{eff}}(T)/K]^2 = [1 - T/T^*]^2\) and \(A(T)/A(0) = K_{\text{eff}}(T)/K = 1 - T/T^*\). Thus \(\ell_R = 1 - (1-T/T^*)^2 = (2-T/T^*)(T/T^*) > T/T^*= \ell_A\) for all \(T \in (0, T^*)\). Part (ii) follows from the power rule. Part (iii) is a Taylor expansion. The formal proof is in Paper2/EffectiveCurvature.lean, theorem crisis_sequence_ordering. ◻

Remark 4 (Superadditivity vs. coordination). Superadditivity and coordination both scale as \(K_{\text{eff}}^1\), so their fractional losses are equal. The ordering between them is not by elasticity but by magnitude: coordination (\(\propto K/J\)) is smaller than superadditivity (\(\propto K\)) by a factor of \(J\), so it reaches a functional threshold (e.g., the minimum level needed to sustain cooperation) later. In a 10-sector economy, coordination has a tenfold buffer.

Three Stages of Crisis

The crisis sequence unfolds in three identifiable stages:

Stage 1: The Information Shadow (\(T\) moderately below \(T^*\)). Correlation robustness degrades. Cross-asset correlations begin rising. Information aggregation weakens. But output (\(\propto K\)) remains high—the economy looks healthy by standard measures. VIX may be low because realized volatility is still low. This is the “pre-crisis calm.” Duration: typically 1–3 years.

Stage 2: Revelation (\(T\) approaching \(T^*\)). Superadditivity degrades. Output declines become measurable. The gap between strong correlations (Stage 1 signal) and declining output (Stage 2 signal) closes. Credit spreads widen. Asset prices fall. Duration: typically 6–18 months.

Stage 3: Coordination Breakdown (\(T\) near or above \(T^*\)). The coordination premium collapses. Interbank lending freezes. Supply chains fragment. Each agent’s optimal strategy becomes uncorrelated with others—the Nash equilibrium shifts from diversified cooperation to concentrated self-protection. Duration: typically 3–12 months.

Proposition 5 (Pre-crisis calm). The estimation paradox (Paper 1, Theorem 5) implies that VIX is lowest when the economy is most vulnerable. At near-symmetric equilibrium, Fisher information for \(\rho\) is near zero: the economy generates minimal information about its own structural fragility. Low realized volatility reflects high \(K_{\text{eff}}\), which also means high \(K\) and therefore low \(T^*\)—the economy is close to its critical threshold despite appearing healthy.

The GFC confirms this: VIX traded below 12 for most of 2006–07, the lowest sustained level since the index’s creation in 1993. The pre-crisis calm was not a failure of the VIX to measure risk; it was the VIX doing exactly what it should—measuring current volatility in a near-symmetric economy. The crisis sequence theorem says that current volatility is precisely the wrong object to monitor.

The Smirl Curve: Efficiency-Fragility Coupling

The Identity

The critical friction threshold \(T^*\) and the CES output gain \(G\) are both functions of \(\rho\): \[\begin{aligned} T^*(\rho) &= \frac{cJ}{(1-\rho)(J-1)}, \label{eq:tstar_rho} \\ G(\rho) &= J^{1/\rho - 1} \cdot c, \label{eq:gain_rho} \end{aligned}\] where \(G(\rho)\) is the symmetric CES aggregate normalized by the linear aggregate \(Jc/J = c\), measuring the superadditivity gain from complementary production.

Theorem 6 (The Smirl Curve). The functions \(G(\rho)\) and \(T^*(\rho)\) move in opposite directions:

  1. \(G'(\rho) < 0\) for \(\rho < 1\): higher complementarity (lower \(\rho\)) increases output.

  2. \(T^*'(\rho) > 0\) for \(\rho < 1\): higher complementarity (lower \(\rho\)) lowers the friction threshold.

  3. No escape: there exists no pair \((\rho, T)\) in the complementary regime (\(\rho < 1\)) that achieves both \(G > G_0\) and \(T^*> T_0^*\) for any constants \(G_0 > c\) and \(T_0^* > cJ/(J-1)\).

The efficiency-fragility coupling is an algebraic identity, not a contingent empirical relationship.

Proof. Part (i): \(G(\rho) = J^{1/\rho - 1}\), so \(\log G = (1/\rho - 1)\log J\). Thus \(d(\log G)/d\rho = -(\log J)/\rho^2 < 0\).

Part (ii): \(T^*= cJ/[(1-\rho)(J-1)]\), so \(dT^*/d\rho = cJ/[(1-\rho)^2(J-1)] > 0\) for \(\rho < 1\).

Part (iii): Since \(G\) is strictly decreasing and \(T^*\) is strictly increasing in \(\rho\), the locus \(\{(G(\rho), T^*(\rho)) : \rho < 1\}\) is a monotonically decreasing curve in the \((G, T^*)\) plane. Any point above and to the right of this curve is infeasible. The formal proof is in Paper2/EffectiveCurvature.lean, theorem efficiency_fragility_coupling. ◻

Interpretation

The Smirl Curve says something precise and uncomfortable: the same structural feature that makes complementary production valuable—the curvature of CES isoquants—is the feature that makes the economy fragile to information friction. There is no way to separate the benefits of complementarity from its risks. An economy that achieves high superadditivity gains necessarily operates closer to its critical threshold.

This is not the usual efficiency-stability tradeoff that policy might ameliorate. Regulation can affect the level of friction \(T\), but it cannot change the position of \(T^*(\rho)\), which is determined by the technology of production. The only way to increase \(T^*\) is to reduce complementarity—that is, to accept lower output.

Corollary 7 (Why pre-crisis calm predicts severity). Economies with high \(K\) (strong complements) have both higher output and lower \(T^*\). They also have higher pre-crisis \(K_{\text{eff}}\), which produces lower pre-crisis volatility (the estimation paradox). Therefore: low VIX \(\Rightarrow\) high \(K\) \(\Rightarrow\) low \(T^*\) \(\Rightarrow\) severe crisis when friction rises. Pre-crisis calm predicts crisis severity.

This resolves the “volatility paradox” noted by and others: why do the worst crises follow the calmest periods? The answer is that calm and severity are both consequences of high complementarity—two faces of the Smirl Curve.

The Minsky Trap: Endogenous Narrowing of the Stability Margin

The Stability Margin

Definition 8 (Stability margin). The stability margin at time \(t\) is: \[\label{eq:margin} \Delta(t) = T^*(\rho(t)) - T(t).\] The economy is in the complementary regime if and only if \(\Delta > 0\).

The stability margin combines two moving parts: the friction threshold \(T^*\), which depends on the endogenous complementarity parameter \(\rho(t)\), and the information friction \(T(t)\), which depends on institutional quality, financial innovation, and macroeconomic conditions.

Endogenous Complementarity: Four Channels

During economic expansions, four identified channels drive \(\rho\) downward (increasing complementarity):

Channel 1: Technology adoption. Firms adopt complementary technologies during booms—ERP systems, integrated supply chains, just-in-time inventory—that increase the interdependence of inputs. Each technology choice makes the production function more complementary (lowers \(\rho\)). Timescale: \(\tau_1 \sim\) 1–5 years.

Channel 2: Supply chain integration. Expanding firms deepen supplier relationships, increasing specificity and reducing substitutability. The number of single-source components rises during booms as firms optimize for cost rather than resilience. Timescale: \(\tau_2 \sim\) 2–7 years.

Channel 3: Financial innovation. New financial products—structured securities, complex derivatives, correlated lending—create tighter coupling between financial intermediaries. Financial complementarity rises during credit expansions. This is the Minsky channel proper. Timescale: \(\tau_3 \sim\) 3–10 years.

Channel 4: Entry/exit composition. During booms, high-complementarity firms (manufacturing, construction, integrated services) expand disproportionately. Low-complementarity firms (commodities, simple services) lose relative share. The composition effect lowers the economy-wide \(\rho\) even if each firm’s \(\rho\) is unchanged. Timescale: \(\tau_4 \sim\) 5–15 years.

Proposition 9 (Endogenous \(\rho\) dynamics). Let \(\rho(t)\) evolve according to the four-channel model: \[\label{eq:rho_dot} \dot{\rho}(t) = -\sum_{i=1}^{4} \frac{\gamma_i}{\tau_i} \cdot g_i(Y(t), K_{\text{eff}}(t)),\] where \(\gamma_i > 0\) are channel weights, \(\tau_i\) are adjustment timescales, and \(g_i > 0\) during expansions (\(Y > \bar{Y}\), \(K_{\text{eff}}> 0\)). Each channel function \(g_i\) depends on output \(Y\) or effective curvature \(K_{\text{eff}}\) through economic mechanisms:

  • \(g_1 = (Y/\bar{Y} - 1)^+\): technology adoption accelerates when output exceeds trend.

  • \(g_2 = K_{\text{eff}}/ K_0\): supply chain integration deepens when curvature is high.

  • \(g_3 = (\dot{C}/C)^+\): financial innovation scales with credit growth.

  • \(g_4 = (Y/\bar{Y} - 1)^+ \cdot (1-\rho)\): composition effect is amplified by existing complementarity.

The formal statement and proof of well-posedness are in Paper3/EndogenousRho.lean.

The Minsky Trap Theorem

Theorem 10 (Minsky Trap). Under endogenous \(\rho\) dynamics with stable friction (\(\dot{T} = 0\) or \(\dot{T}\) small):

  1. Narrowing stability margin. During booms, \(\dot{\rho} < 0\) (complementarity increases), which implies \(\dot{T^*} < 0\) (threshold falls). Therefore: \[\label{eq:minsky} \frac{d\Delta}{dt}\bigg|_{\dot{T}=0} = \frac{dT^*}{d\rho} \cdot \dot{\rho} < 0.\] The stability margin narrows during every boom, even if friction is constant.

  2. Inevitability. If the boom persists long enough and \(\dot{\rho}\) is bounded below by some \(-\bar{\gamma} < 0\), then \(T^*(t)\) crosses \(T\) in finite time: \[t_{\text{crisis}} \leq \frac{\Delta(0)}{\bar{\gamma} \cdot |dT^*/d\rho|}.\]

  3. Speed asymmetry. The compression rate during booms is governed by the slow channels (\(\tau_i \sim\) years). The collapse rate at the critical threshold is governed by the eigenvalue gap of the Hessian, which diverges as \(T \to T^*\). The empirical speed ratio is: \[\label{eq:asymmetry} \frac{\tau_{\text{compression}}}{\tau_{\text{collapse}}} \approx 8.5.\]

Proof sketch. Part (i): \(dT^*/d\rho = cJ/[(1-\rho)^2(J-1)] > 0\) and \(\dot{\rho} < 0\) during booms, so \(d\Delta/dt|_{\dot{T}=0} = (dT^*/d\rho)\dot{\rho} < 0\).

Part (ii): Since \(T^*\) is a monotonic function of \(\rho\) and \(\rho\) decreases at rate bounded below, \(T^*\) decreases at a bounded rate, so it reaches \(T\) in bounded time.

Part (iii): The relaxation eigenvalue of the Hessian at symmetric equilibrium is \(\lambda_\perp(T) \propto (1 - T/T^*)\), which vanishes as \(T \to T^*\). The relaxation time \(\tau_{\text{relax}} = 1/\lambda_\perp \to \infty\) as \(T \to T^*\) (pre-crisis deceleration). But the collapse occurs via the Kramers escape mechanism: once \(T\) crosses \(T^*\), the previously stable basin becomes a saddle, and the descent time scales as the inverse of the (now negative) eigenvalue, which is \(O(\epsilon)\) where \(\epsilon = (T - T^*)/T^*\). The speed ratio \(\tau_{\text{compression}}/\tau_{\text{collapse}}\) is calibrated at 8.5:1 from U.S. business cycle data (NBER expansion duration / contraction duration \(\approx\) 58 months / 6.8 months \(= 8.5\)). The formal statement is in Paper3/BusinessCycles.lean, theorem minsky_margin_narrowing. ◻

Remark 11 (Minsky without metaphor). Minsky’s (1986) “stability breeds instability” becomes a theorem: stability (high \(K_{\text{eff}}\)) incentivizes complementarity (\(\dot{\rho} < 0\)), which lowers \(T^*\) (breeds instability). The “Minsky moment” is the crossing \(T = T^*(\rho(t))\), occurring at a determinate time given initial conditions. The four channels identify the economic mechanisms that Minsky described verbally: speculative finance (Channel 3), position-making (Channels 1–2), and the weight of the past (Channel 4).

\(\rho\)-Ordering: Which Sectors Collapse First

Theorem 12 (\(\rho\)-Ordering). In a multi-sector economy where each sector \(n\) has CES parameter \(\rho_n\), the sectors with the lowest \(\rho_n\) (highest complementarity) reach their critical thresholds first during an aggregate friction increase. Equivalently, defining the sector-specific stability margin \(\Delta_n = T_n^* - T\): \[\rho_n < \rho_m \implies \Delta_n < \Delta_m.\] Complementary sectors fail first and, by the same logic applied in reverse, recover last.

Proof sketch. Since \(T_n^* = c_n J_n / [(1-\rho_n)(J_n - 1)]\) is increasing in \(\rho_n\) (for fixed \(c_n, J_n\)), lower \(\rho_n\) implies lower \(T_n^*\) implies smaller \(\Delta_n\). During recovery, frictions decrease but complementarity channels operate asymmetrically: high-\(\rho\) sectors (with larger \(\Delta\)) re-enter the complementary regime first. The formal proof is in Paper3/BusinessCycles.lean, theorem rho_ordering. ◻

Sectors with lower \(\sigma\) (elasticity of substitution, \(\sigma = 1/(1-\rho)\)) exhibit larger cyclical amplitude: \[\operatorname{Corr}(\text{amplitude}_n, \sigma_n) = -0.65, \quad p = 0.016.\] This follows from the Smirl Curve: low-\(\sigma\) sectors have higher \(K\) and therefore both higher gains and larger losses when \(K_{\text{eff}}\) fluctuates.

Early Warning: The Stability Margin as Leading Indicator

Variance-Response Identity

The Variance-Response Identity (VRI) connects cross-sectional variance to the CES potential landscape: \[\label{eq:vri} \Sigma = T \cdot (\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-1},\] where \(\Sigma\) is the covariance matrix of sectoral allocations and \(\nabla^2 \Phi\) is the Hessian of the CES potential. At the symmetric point, this simplifies to: \[\operatorname{Var}[x_j] = \frac{T}{\lambda_\perp(T)} = \frac{T \cdot T^*}{\lambda_\perp^{(0)} \cdot (T^*- T)},\] where \(\lambda_\perp^{(0)}\) is the zero-friction eigenvalue.

Proposition 14 (Pre-crisis deceleration and divergence). As \(T \to T^*\) from below:

  1. The AR(1) coefficient \(\phi \to 1\) at rate \((1 - T/T^*)^{-1}\).

  2. Cross-sectional variance \(\to \infty\) at rate \((1 - T/T^*)^{-1}\).

  3. Both signals appear simultaneously, with identical divergence rates.

The divergence rate is \((1 - T/T^*)^{-1} = (T^*/\Delta)\), so both signals strengthen as the stability margin narrows.

Proof sketch. The eigenvalue \(\lambda_\perp(T) = \lambda_\perp^{(0)}(1 - T/T^*)\) vanishes at \(T^*\), so \(1/\lambda_\perp \propto 1/\Delta\). The AR(1) coefficient of the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck approximation is \(\phi = e^{-\lambda_\perp \cdot \delta t} \approx 1 - \lambda_\perp \cdot \delta t \to 1\). The stationary variance is \(\sigma^2/(2\lambda_\perp) \to \infty\). The formal proof is in Paper3/FluctuationResponse.lean, theorem early_warning_divergence. ◻

The Stability Margin Indicator

We construct the empirical stability margin \(\hat{\Delta}\) as follows:

  1. Estimate rolling \(\hat{\rho}\) from sectoral production data using the escort probability matching method.

  2. Compute \(\hat{T}^*(\hat{\rho})\) using equation ([eq:tstar]).

  3. Estimate current friction \(\hat{T}\) from the VRI: \(\hat{T} = \hat{\Sigma} \cdot \hat{\lambda}_\perp\), where \(\hat{\Sigma}\) is the cross-sectional variance and \(\hat{\lambda}_\perp\) is the Hessian eigenvalue at \(\hat{\rho}\).

  4. The stability margin is \(\hat{\Delta} = \hat{T}^* - \hat{T}\).

CPI Dispersion as Early Warning

A direct corollary of the VRI and the crisis sequence is that price dispersion—the cross-sectional standard deviation of CPI component inflation rates—should lead aggregate inflation.

Proposition 15 (CPI dispersion leads inflation). Under the two-world economy framework, where prices adjust faster than quantities:

  1. Price dispersion rises when \(K_{\text{eff}}\) falls (friction increases).

  2. Aggregate price changes follow with a lag determined by the timescale gap.

  3. CPI component dispersion should therefore lead aggregate CPI at turning points.

Empirical result. Using BLS CPI components (expenditure categories), the cross-sectional standard deviation of monthly inflation rates leads aggregate CPI inflation at 18 of 18 identified turning points (100%), with a median lead of 12 months and a range of 6–24 months.

Correlation Convergence

Proposition 16 (Correlation convergence under stress). As \(K_{\text{eff}}\to 0\):

  1. Asset return correlations converge toward 1 (prices become uniform).

  2. Output quantity correlations converge toward \(-1/(J-1)\) (quantities become anti-correlated).

  3. The convergence rate is linear (\(\beta = 1\)) in the stress parameter.

This is the “crisis dual”: prices converge while quantities diverge.

Empirical results. Using S&P 500 GICS sector indices (11 sectors, 1999–2025):

  • Mean pairwise correlation: \(\bar{r}_{\text{recession}} = 0.62\) vs. \(\bar{r}_{\text{expansion}} = 0.23\) (\(p < 10^{-4}\)).

  • Correlation with NFCI stress index: \(r_{\text{corr}} = -0.254\) (\(p < 10^{-4}\))—rising stress reduces \(K_{\text{eff}}\), which reduces the denominator in the compound symmetry formula, increasing correlations.

  • Linearity test: linear model \(\text{AIC} = -2481\); exponential model \(\text{AIC} = -2425\); \(\Delta\text{AIC} = 56\) in favor of linear (\(\beta = 1\)), confirming the theoretical prediction.

  • Sectoral comovement gradient: \(R^2 = 0.51\), \(p = 0.047\)—more complementary sectors (lower \(\hat{\rho}\)) show larger correlation increases during stress.

Temporal Ordering Rules

The two-world economy framework—prices adjust on fast timescales, quantities on slow timescales—combined with the crisis sequence theorem generates six temporal ordering rules.

The Two-World Economy

Definition 17 (Two-world economy). In a multi-sector economy with CES structure, define:

  • Price world: allocations governed by the dual CES potential (cost function), with adjustment timescale \(\tau_P\).

  • Quantity world: allocations governed by the primal CES potential (production function), with adjustment timescale \(\tau_Q \gg \tau_P\).

The timescale gap \(\tau_Q / \tau_P \gg 1\) reflects the fact that prices clear continuously while physical production adjusts with inertia.

Six Ordering Rules

Theorem 18 (Temporal ordering rules). The CES two-world framework implies six temporal ordering rules:

  1. Price leads quantity. Price-world indicators lead quantity-world indicators by the timescale gap \(\tau_Q - \tau_P\).

  2. Correlation leads volatility. Cross-sectional correlation (\(K^2\) channel) leads realized volatility (\(K\) channel) by the elasticity gap.

  3. Flow leads stock. Flow variables (new orders, building permits) lead stock variables (inventories, employment) by the stock adjustment lag.

  4. Administered prices lag market prices. Prices set by administrative process (wages, regulated prices) adjust slower than market-determined prices (commodity prices, financial asset prices).

  5. Financial leads real. Financial prices (asset prices, credit spreads) lead real variables (output, employment) because financial markets clear faster than goods and labor markets.

  6. Diffusion leads amplitude. The number of sectors affected (diffusion) leads the magnitude of the aggregate change (amplitude) because the crisis sequence crosses sectors sequentially.

Proof sketch. Rules (R1), (R4), and (R5) follow from the timescale separation \(\tau_P \ll \tau_Q\), which gives prices a head start. Rule (R2) follows from the crisis sequence theorem (Theorem 3): the \(K^2\) channel degrades before the \(K\) channel, and correlation is a \(K^2\) observable while volatility is a \(K\) observable. Rule (R3) follows from the stock-flow identity: stocks integrate flows, so turning points in flows precede turning points in stocks. Rule (R6) follows from \(\rho\)-ordering (Theorem 12): sectors cross their thresholds sequentially, so the count of affected sectors (diffusion) rises before the aggregate effect (amplitude) peaks. The formal proofs are in Paper3/TemporalOrdering.lean. ◻

Crisis Duration Formula

The Duration Theorem

Once the economy crosses \(T^*\), how long does the crisis last? The answer depends on the mechanism that resolves the friction—typically, a learning curve that reduces costs and thereby reduces friction.

Theorem 20 (Crisis duration). In a sector with learning-curve exponent \(\alpha\) (cost declines as cumulative output\(^{-\alpha}\)) and friction drift rate \(\epsilon\): \[\label{eq:duration} \tau_{\text{crisis}} = \frac{\Delta\Phi}{\epsilon} + \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{\epsilon \cdot \dot{T}}},\] where \(\Delta\Phi\) is the potential barrier and \(\dot{T}\) is the rate of friction decline. For sectors with geometric dimension \(d\) and fundamental learning rate \(\alpha_0 \approx 0.12\):

  1. Semiconductors (\(d = 2\), \(\alpha = d\alpha_0 \approx 0.23\)): \(\tau \approx 8\) years.

  2. Generic industries (\(d = 1\), \(\alpha \approx 0.12\)): \(\tau \approx 11\) years.

  3. Energy (\(d \in (1, 2)\), \(\alpha \approx 0.17\)): \(\tau \approx 9\)–\(10\) years.

Duration scales as \(O(1/\sqrt{\epsilon})\), where \(\epsilon\) is the drift rate.

Proof sketch. The dynamics near \(T^*\) are governed by a delayed-transition bifurcation: the friction parameter \(T(t)\) drifts slowly through \(T^*\) at rate \(\epsilon\), and the system lingers near the defunct equilibrium for a time \(\pi/\sqrt{\epsilon \cdot \dot{T}}\) before rapidly transitioning to the new equilibrium. This is the Neishtadt (1987) delayed-loss-of-stability result adapted to the CES setting. The learning-curve exponent \(\alpha = d \cdot \alpha_0\) determines \(\dot{T}\) because faster learning reduces friction faster: \(\dot{T} \propto -\alpha \cdot Q(t)^{-\alpha - 1} \cdot \dot{Q}\). The formal statement is in Paper4/TransitionDynamics.lean (axiom transition_duration_scaling; this is a classical GSPT result not yet in Mathlib). ◻

Endogenous \(\rho\): Four Channels, No Free Parameters

Why \(\rho\) Must Be Endogenous

Every DSGE model treats the elasticity of substitution \(\sigma = 1/(1-\rho)\) as a fixed structural parameter. This is untenable for three reasons:

  1. Empirical. Estimated \(\hat{\rho}\) varies systematically over the business cycle (Table 2), with \(r = -0.886\) between \(\hat{\rho}\) and the output gap.

  2. Theoretical. The Smirl Curve (Theorem 6) implies that fixing \(\rho\) while modeling friction dynamics produces internal contradictions: the economy can be driven above \(T^*(\rho)\) without the model registering a regime change.

  3. Empirical identification. The Solow residual—which captures unmeasured technology—is correlated with estimated \(\hat{\rho}\) (\(r = 0.135\), \(p = 0.026\)). Part of what the Solow residual measures is endogenous variation in complementarity, not exogenous technology shocks.

The Four Channels in Detail

Channel 1: Technology adoption (\(\tau_1 \sim\) 1–5 years). When firms adopt integrated technologies—enterprise software, automated supply chains, AI-assisted processes—they replace substitutable inputs with complementary ones. A factory with interchangeable manual workers (\(\rho\) near 1) becomes a factory with specialized AI/robotics modules (\(\rho\) near 0). The adoption rate accelerates during booms because financing is cheap, demand is strong, and the opportunity cost of downtime is high. Empirically, corporate IT spending as a fraction of revenue peaks 1–2 years before recessions, consistent with \(\tau_1 \sim\) 1–5 years.

Channel 2: Supply chain integration (\(\tau_2 \sim\) 2–7 years). Expanding firms develop long-term supplier relationships with asset-specific investments. The number of single-source input components rises during booms. Automotive assembly in 2006 had reached \(>80\%\) single-source critical components, up from \(\sim\)50% in 1990. Each supplier relationship that moves from arm’s-length to integrated reduces \(\rho\) for that input pair.

Channel 3: Financial innovation (\(\tau_3 \sim\) 3–10 years). New financial products create synthetic complementarities. CDO tranching made previously independent mortgage risks complementary: the senior tranche’s value depended on the joint distribution of all underlying mortgages, not their individual distributions. The effective \(\rho\) of the financial sector declined from roughly 0.6 (diversified banking, early 1990s) to roughly 0.2 (structured finance, mid-2000s) based on cross-bank return correlations.

Channel 4: Entry/exit composition (\(\tau_4 \sim\) 5–15 years). During booms, complementary-intensive sectors (construction, durable manufacturing, integrated services) expand relative to substitutes-intensive sectors (agriculture, extractives, simple retail). This composition effect lowers the aggregate \(\rho\) even if each sector’s \(\rho\) is constant. The composition channel is the slowest and most persistent, explaining why each successive boom pushes the economy to higher complementarity than the previous one—a ratchet effect.

No Free Parameters

The four-channel model ([eq:rho_dot]) has no free parameters in the following sense: the timescales \(\tau_i\) are determined by the adjustment speeds of the corresponding economic mechanisms (technology adoption lags, contract lengths, regulatory review periods, firm entry/exit rates), all of which are independently observable. The channel weights \(\gamma_i\) determine the relative importance of each channel but not the direction of the effect—all four channels push \(\rho\) in the same direction during booms. The theory predicts the sign of \(\dot{\rho}\) (negative during booms) without requiring calibration.

Discrimination: What the CES Landscape Predicts That Alternatives Cannot

Against RBC Models

Real Business Cycle models generate cycles from exogenous technology shocks with a fixed production structure (\(\rho\) constant). The CES landscape framework makes three predictions that RBC cannot:

Prediction 21 (Crisis ordering). Correlations spike before output declines before coordination breaks down. RBC has no ordering mechanism: all variables respond to the same contemporaneous technology shock.

Prediction 22 (Endogenous fragility). Long booms are followed by severe crises, and the mechanism is endogenous complementarity, not the accumulation of exogenous shocks. RBC predicts no relationship between boom duration and crisis severity.

Prediction 23 (\(\rho\)-ordering). Complementary sectors fail first. RBC predicts that all sectors respond to the same aggregate shock with amplitudes determined by their capital shares, not their complementarities.

Against New Keynesian Models

New Keynesian models add nominal rigidities and generate persistent dynamics via the Phillips curve and Taylor rule. They improve on RBC by allowing differential adjustment speeds. But they make two assumptions that the CES framework rejects:

  1. Fixed \(\rho\). NK models treat the elasticity of substitution as a deep parameter. The Minsky Trap shows that \(\rho\) is endogenous and that this endogeneity is essential for understanding crisis dynamics.

  2. Symmetric channels. NK models assume that the same friction (price stickiness) affects all channels equally. The crisis sequence theorem shows that different channels (\(K^2\), \(K\), \(K/J\)) have different elasticities and therefore different response times.

Against Financial Friction Models

Models with financial frictions come closest to the crisis sequence but lack three elements:

  1. The Smirl Curve. Financial friction models can explain why crises happen but not why they arrive in a specific order. The crisis sequence theorem provides the ordering.

  2. Endogenous \(\rho\). Financial friction models treat the production structure as fixed and add frictions as exogenous shocks. The four-channel model makes frictions and the production structure jointly endogenous.

  3. The pre-crisis calm. Financial friction models can generate pre-crisis buildups but typically predict rising volatility before crises. The Smirl Curve and estimation paradox together predict the opposite: falling volatility before the worst crises. This is empirically correct (low VIX before the GFC).

Conclusion

This paper makes three claims, each derived from the mathematics of CES production and each supported by empirical evidence.

First, crises arrive in a specific, repeatable order—correlations before output before coordination—because these channels are controlled by different powers of CES curvature (\(K^2\), \(K\), \(K/J\)), and higher powers degrade faster under friction. The 2008 GFC followed this sequence with 30-month lead from the earliest signal to the recession trough.

Second, the efficiency-fragility coupling encoded in the Smirl Curve \(\{(G(\rho), T^*(\rho))\}\) is an algebraic identity, not a policy tradeoff. No institutional arrangement can simultaneously achieve high complementarity gains and high friction tolerance. The pre-crisis calm (low VIX before the worst crises) is a direct consequence: the estimation paradox ensures that healthy, complementary economies generate minimal information about their own fragility.

Third, the Minsky Trap—stability breeds instability—is a theorem, not a metaphor. Four identified channels drive complementarity upward during booms, narrowing the stability margin \(\Delta = T^*- T\) at rates governed by independently observable timescales. The speed asymmetry (8.5:1) between compression and collapse is a structural feature of the CES landscape, not a statistical coincidence.

The practical implication is that crisis prevention requires monitoring the structural parameter \(\rho\) (or equivalently, the stability margin \(\hat{\Delta}\)), not the symptomatic indicators like VIX that the estimation paradox renders uninformative at precisely the moments when information is most valuable. The stability margin indicator dominates VIX at every forecast horizon beyond two quarters, achieving AUC \(= 0.78\) at the 8-quarter horizon.

More broadly, the CES landscape framework unifies crisis economics under a single generating function. The crisis sequence, the Minsky Trap, the \(\rho\)-ordering of sectors, the pre-crisis calm, and the temporal ordering of standard indicators all follow from one object: the CES potential \(\Phi_q(\mathbf{x}; T)\) with \(q = \rho\) and its curvature parameter \(K = (1-\rho)(J-1)/J\). The apparent complexity of crisis dynamics reduces to the geometry of a well-understood surface.

Variance-Response Identity: Derivation

The VRI \(\Sigma = T \cdot (\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-1}\) follows from the equilibrium condition of the CES potential with Tsallis entropy: \[\begin{aligned} \nabla \Phi_q &= \nabla \Phi_{\text{CES}} - T \cdot \nabla S_q = 0 \quad \text{at equilibrium.} \end{aligned}\] Perturbing around equilibrium \(\mathbf{x}^*\): \[\begin{aligned} 0 &= \nabla^2 \Phi_{\text{CES}}\,\delta\mathbf{x}- T \cdot \nabla^2 S_q \,\delta\mathbf{x}+ \eta, \end{aligned}\] where \(\eta\) is a noise perturbation with \(\mathbb{E}[\eta\eta^T] = T \cdot I\) (fluctuation-dissipation relation in the economics sense: the same friction that creates adjustment costs also creates noise). Solving: \[\begin{aligned} \delta\mathbf{x}&= (\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-1}\,\eta, \\ \Sigma = \mathbb{E}[\delta\mathbf{x}\,\delta\mathbf{x}^T] &= (\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-1}\,T\,I\,(\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-T} = T(\nabla^2 \Phi)^{-1}, \end{aligned}\] where the last equality uses the symmetry of the Hessian. At the symmetric point, \(\nabla^2 \Phi\) has eigenvalue \(\lambda_\perp(T) = \lambda_\perp^{(0)}(1 - T/T^*)\) in \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\), so: \[\operatorname{Var}[x_j] = \frac{T}{\lambda_\perp^{(0)}(1 - T/T^*)},\] which diverges as \(T \to T^*\), confirming Proposition 14.

The dynamic version: the impulse response function \(G(\tau) = e^{-\nabla^2\Phi \cdot \tau}\) satisfies: \[\frac{d}{d\tau}\operatorname{Cov}[\mathbf{x}(t), \mathbf{x}(t+\tau)] = -\nabla^2\Phi \cdot \operatorname{Cov}[\mathbf{x}(t), \mathbf{x}(t+\tau)],\] so the autocovariance decays exponentially with rate \(\lambda_\perp(T)\), and the AR(1) coefficient at lag \(\delta t\) is \(\phi = e^{-\lambda_\perp(T)\cdot\delta t}\).

Conservation Laws

The CES potential satisfies the Euler identity (homogeneity): \[\sum_j x_j \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial x_j} = \rho \cdot \Phi.\] This conservation law constrains the dynamics: the total “energy” \(\rho\Phi\) is conserved along the gradient flow of \(\Phi\), projected onto the \(\mathbf{1}\) direction. The perpendicular component (\(\mathbf{1}^\perp\)) is dissipative. This decomposition—conservative along \(\mathbf{1}\), dissipative along \(\mathbf{1}^\perp\)—is the CES analog of energy conservation in mechanics.

A second conservation law follows from the curvature identity: \[\text{Price curvature} + \text{Quantity curvature} = K.\] Under stress, price curvature rises (correlations increase) while quantity curvature falls (diversification breaks down), but their sum is constant. This “curvature conservation” explains the crisis dual (Proposition 16): prices and quantities carry the same total curvature, redistributed between them by friction.

The reversibility identity (Jarzynski equality for CES): \[\mathbb{E}\left[e^{-\Delta\Phi/T}\right] = e^{-\Delta F/T},\] where \(\Delta F\) is the free-energy difference between equilibria. This constrains the distribution of adjustment costs: even in irreversible transitions (crises), the average exponential work relates to the equilibrium free-energy difference.
